face

A colleague suggests increasing carvedilol and enalapril because the patient has HFrEF. Is that safe with this BP and creatinine trend?

smart_toy

With SBP 89 mmHg + dizziness + cool peripheries + rising creatinine, it is not safe to up‑titrate enalapril or carvedilol right now—this pattern suggests poor perfusion/cardiogenic shock physiology and/or acute kidney injury, and both UCG and the GDMT tables emphasize caution/holding titration when SBP is <90 mmHg and when renal function is worsening [UCG 4.1.3], [GDMT Tables]. The HF Algorithm also flags cardiogenic shock as a red flag needing urgent attention [HF Algorithm].

Medication safety issues to flag before any HF medicine change (and to communicate to the doctor/cardiologist):

Enalapril (ACEi): UCG cautions ACEi use when SBP <90 and to monitor renal function [UCG 4.1.3]. GDMT tables: ACEi titration requires SBP >90, and hold if creatinine rises >30% or symptomatic hypotension; also hold if K ≥5.5 [GDMT Tables].
Carvedilol (beta blocker): UCG cautions beta blockers when SBP <90 [UCG 4.1.3]. GDMT tables: do not uptitrate carvedilol unless SBP >90 (and HR >55), and avoid uptitration if the patient is volume overloaded/decompensated [GDMT Tables].
Spironolactone (MRA): needs close K/Cr monitoring; GDMT tables: requires eGFR >30 and K <5.0, and hold if K ≥5.5 [GDMT Tables].
Furosemide: if the creatinine rise is from hypoperfusion/AKI, aggressive diuresis can worsen renal function; if it’s from congestion, IV diuresis may be needed—so the direction depends on volume status and requires senior review.

Escalation/decision safety rule: any holding, stopping, starting, or dose change of these HF medicines (including “skipping today’s doses”) requires cardiologist/doctor confirmation before administration.

Single next step (now): treat this as possible shock/AKI and get an immediate senior doctor/cardiologist decision while you document perfusion status—specifically record heart rate, capillary refill, mental state, respiratory rate, SpO₂, and urine output (UCG shock clinical features) [UCG 1.1.2].

Focused question (to decide if this is cardiogenic shock vs over-diuresis/other cause, and what to tell the doctor):
What are his HR, SpO₂, capillary refill time, mental status, and urine output (or time since last urine), and on exam is he congested (JVP raised/basal crackles/leg oedema) or dry? [UCG 1.1.2], [HF Algorithm]
