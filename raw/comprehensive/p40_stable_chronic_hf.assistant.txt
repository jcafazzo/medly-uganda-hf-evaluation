While you’re checking those vitals/labs, here is a patient-friendly explanation you can use in clinic (no dosing changes implied):

How to explain her key HF medicines (plain language)
Furosemide (“water pill”): helps the kidneys remove extra salt and water, reducing leg swelling and breathlessness; she should expect periodic blood tests to monitor salts and kidney function [PocketDoktor].
Enalapril (ACE inhibitor): helps open blood vessels and reduces salt/water retention, making it easier for the heart to pump; watch for symptoms of low BP (dizziness) and (for ACE‑i) a dry cough [PocketDoktor].
Carvedilol (beta blocker): blocks adrenaline so the heart doesn’t work as hard or beat as fast; warn her to report dizziness or very slow pulse, and not to stop it suddenly unless told by a clinician [PocketDoktor].
Spironolactone (MRA): helps prevent scarring/stiffness of the heart; explain that she needs ongoing kidney function and potassium blood tests while on it [PocketDoktor].
Lifestyle advice that matters most (UCG + patient-friendly framing)
Salt + fluids: reduce salt intake and limit fluids to ~1–1.5 L/day if advised as part of chronic HF care [UCG 4.1.3]. Explain “too much salt makes the body hold water, worsening swelling and breathlessness” [PocketDoktor].
Alcohol: limit/avoid alcohol as it can worsen HF [UCG 4.1.3], [PocketDoktor].
Activity: encourage regular exercise within her symptom limits (pace activity; stop if very breathless, dizzy, or chest pain) [UCG 4.1.3], [PocketDoktor].
Home monitoring: teach her to track weight and symptoms; seek care if weight rises >1 kg overnight or 2–3 kg in a week, or if breathlessness/swelling/dizziness worsens [PocketDoktor]. (These are “Seek Help” warning-type changes.)
Monitoring at follow-ups (what you, the clinic, should check)

UCG recommends periodic monitoring of weight, BP, HR, RR, and oxygen saturation in chronic HF [UCG 4.1.3]. The HF algorithm also emphasizes checking renal function tests during ongoing care [HF Algorithm].
