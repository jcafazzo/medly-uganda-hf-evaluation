A colleague suggests increasing carvedilol and enalapril because the patient has HFrEF. Is that safe with this BP and creatinine trend?

smart_toy

With SBP 84 mmHg + cool peripheries + rising creatinine, this is a red flag for cardiogenic shock/poor perfusion, so up‑titrating carvedilol or enalapril is not safe right now. The HF Algorithm flags cardiogenic shock as an urgent red flag [HF Algorithm]. UCG also cautions to use ACE inhibitors and beta blockers with caution when SBP <90 mmHg and to monitor renal function [UCG 4.1.3]. The GDMT tables specify SBP >90 mmHg as a hemodynamic requirement for enalapril and carvedilol up‑titration [GDMT Tables].

Medication safety issue to flag immediately: avoid any up‑titration of:

Enalapril (ACEi) in symptomatic hypotension and creatinine rise; GDMT tables also advise holding if creatinine rises >30% from baseline or symptomatic hypotension [GDMT Tables].
Carvedilol (beta blocker): GDMT tables require SBP >90 and HR >55 for titration, and “do not uptitrate if decompensated/volume overloaded” [GDMT Tables].

Any change (increase, decrease, holding, or giving extra acute meds) requires cardiologist/receiving-physician confirmation before administration.

Single next step now (before changing any HF medicines): treat this as shock/unstable HF and escalate immediately to senior clinician/receiving hospital for urgent stabilization and monitored management (including labs/electrolytes, urine output, and causes/triggers) [HF Algorithm; UCG 7.1.1].

To support the escalation call and decide if this looks more like cardiogenic shock vs hypovolaemia/sepsis, what are her heart rate, SpO₂, respiratory rate, and current urine output (ml/hr if catheterized) right now?
