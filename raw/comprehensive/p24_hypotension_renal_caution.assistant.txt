smart_toy

With SBP 89 mmHg + dizziness + cool peripheries and a rising creatinine, it is not safe to up‑titrate enalapril or carvedilol right now. The UCG specifically cautions that ACE inhibitors and beta blockers should be used with caution when systolic BP is <90 mmHg and renal function must be monitored [UCG 4.1.3]. The GDMT tables also set SBP >90 mmHg as a hemodynamic requirement for enalapril and carvedilol titration, and advise holding enalapril if creatinine rises >30% or there is symptomatic hypotension [GDMT Tables]. For carvedilol, titration requires SBP >90 and you should not uptitrate if decompensated/volume overloaded [GDMT Tables].

Medication safety issues to flag before changing anything

Possible shock/poor perfusion (cool peripheries + hypotension + renal deterioration) → treat as a red flag needing urgent escalation [HF Algorithm].
ACE inhibitor (enalapril): risk of worsening hypotension/renal function in this setting; GDMT hold criteria include symptomatic hypotension and Cr rise >30% [GDMT Tables].
Beta blocker (carvedilol): titration requires SBP >90 and avoid titration in decompensation/volume overload [GDMT Tables].
MRA (spironolactone): needs close renal/K monitoring; hold if K ≥5.5 and requires adequate renal function (eGFR >30) [GDMT Tables].
Diuretic (furosemide): if she is oliguric/AKI, UCG ARF guidance notes IV furosemide according to response may be used, often at hospital level with close monitoring and referral if worsening/anuria [UCG 7.1.1]. (This is still a medication administration/change.)

Escalation Given the red flags (hypotension <90, cool peripheries, rising creatinine), the single next step is: escalate immediately to a doctor/senior clinician and arrange urgent transfer to hospital/RR level for management of suspected cardiogenic shock/AKI, rather than titrating chronic HF medicines now [HF Algorithm; UCG 7.1.1].
And to be explicit: any dose increase, holding, or stopping of enalapril/carvedilol/spironolactone/furosemide requires cardiologist/doctor confirmation before administration.

Key clarifying question (to guide immediate stabilization/hand‑over):
Do you have her current SpO₂ and respiratory status (on oxygen or room air), and urine output over the last 6–24 hours (or is she anuric)? [UCG 4.1.4; UCG 7.1.1]
