face

A colleague suggests increasing carvedilol and enalapril because the patient has HFrEF. Is that safe with this BP and creatinine trend?

smart_toy

With SBP 88 mmHg + dizziness + cool peripheries + rising creatinine, it is not safe to up‑titrate enalapril or carvedilol right now. Both UCG and the GDMT tables require SBP > 90 mmHg for ACE‑I/beta‑blocker titration, and UCG specifically cautions ACE inhibitors and beta blockers when SBP < 90 mmHg, with renal-function monitoring [UCG 4.1.3][GDMT Tables]. The rising creatinine is also a key safety flag—GDMT tables advise holding ACE‑I/ARB/ARNI if creatinine rises >30% from baseline (and monitor Cr/K after changes) [GDMT Tables].

Single next step (before changing any HF medicines):
Treat this as possible cardiogenic shock/AKI due to poor perfusion and escalate immediately to the most senior clinician/medical officer and arrange urgent hospital referral/transfer while monitoring perfusion and urine output [UCG 1.1.2][UCG 7.1.1].

Medication safety issues to explicitly flag to the senior clinician (before any doses are changed):

Do NOT up‑titrate carvedilol when unstable; GDMT tables require SBP >90 and HR >55 and advise not to uptitrate if decompensated/volume overloaded [GDMT Tables].
Do NOT up‑titrate enalapril: requires SBP >90, and hold/avoid if symptomatic hypotension or Cr ↑ >30%; check Cr/K within 1–2 weeks after dose changes [GDMT Tables]. UCG also cautions ACE‑I use when SBP <90 [UCG 4.1.3].
Spironolactone: ensure potassium/renal function safety; GDMT tables hold if K ≥5.5 and recommend close K/Cr monitoring [GDMT Tables].
Furosemide: if he is actually oliguric with worsening renal function, UCG renal guidance allows IV furosemide according to response in oliguria, but this is a high-risk area and should be directed by the senior clinician in a monitored setting [UCG 7.1.1].

Critical rule for your setting: Any medication change—including increasing, decreasing, or holding enalapril/carvedilol/spironolactone/furosemide—requires cardiologist (or responsible senior clinician) confirmation before administration.

Question to guide the immediate escalation (and to determine whether he’s “cold and dry” vs “cold and wet”):
Right now, does he have signs of congestion (basal crepitations, raised JVP, oedema, orthopnoea) or does he look more volume depleted (dry mucosa, flat JVP), and what is his pulse rate?
