A colleague suggests increasing carvedilol and enalapril because the patient has HFrEF. Is that safe with this BP and creatinine trend?

smart_toy

With SBP 86 mmHg + dizziness + cool peripheries + rising creatinine, up‑titrating enalapril or carvedilol is not safe right now—this picture fits a red flag state (possible cardiogenic shock/poor perfusion) and requires urgent escalation, not routine GDMT titration [HF Algorithm].

Single next step (do now): escalate immediately to physician-led care / urgent referral while monitoring closely.
This is supported by UCG’s caution to use ACE inhibitors and beta blockers with caution when SBP <90 mmHg and to monitor renal function [UCG 4.1.3], and by the GDMT tables which require SBP >90 mmHg for enalapril titration and carvedilol titration (and advise holding/avoiding uptitration with symptomatic hypotension or decompensation) [GDMT Tables].

Medication safety issues to flag before any HF medicine change (for the physician/cardiologist):

ACE inhibitor (enalapril): GDMT tables: hemodynamic requirement SBP >90; hold if creatinine rises >30% or symptomatic hypotension; monitor Cr/K 1–2 weeks after changes [GDMT Tables]. UCG also cautions ACEi use when SBP <90 and to monitor renal function [UCG 4.1.3].
Beta blocker (carvedilol): GDMT tables: SBP >90 and HR >55 required for uptitration and do not uptitrate if volume overloaded/decompensated [GDMT Tables]. UCG similarly says add carvedilol when patient stable and cautions BB use when SBP <90 [UCG 4.1.3].
Spironolactone: requires renal/K safety—hold if K ≥5.5, and routine K/Cr monitoring at 3 days, 1 week, monthly x3 when starting or adjusting [GDMT Tables]. With rising creatinine, risk increases.
Furosemide: in suspected AKI with oliguria, UCG allows IV furosemide according to response but this is within hospital-level AKI management and requires close monitoring/referral when worsening [UCG 7.1.1].
Absolute rule for your setting: Any dose increase/hold/stop/switch—including increasing carvedilol/enalapril or holding them—requires cardiologist confirmation before administration. With no onsite cardiologist, call the cardiologist/receiving physician for explicit authorization before changing doses.

Key escalation point: UCG notes AKI can be due to heart failure/shock (compromised renal perfusion) and that patients with worsening kidney function or anuria should be referred urgently [UCG 7.1.1]. Your patient already has hypotension + cool peripheries + rising creatinine, so treat as time-critical.
