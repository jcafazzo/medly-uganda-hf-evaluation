With SBP 84 mmHg + cool peripheries + rising creatinine, up‑titrating enalapril or carvedilol is not safe right now—this fits a red‑flag low‑perfusion state (possible cardiogenic shock/decompensated HF with AKI). Both UCG and the GDMT tables set SBP >90 mmHg as a basic requirement and advise caution/holding criteria when hypotensive or when creatinine is worsening [UCG 4.1.3; GDMT Tables; HF Algorithm].

Medication safety issues to flag before any HF medicine change (key ones from your references):

ACE inhibitor (enalapril): UCG cautions ACEi when SBP <90 and says monitor renal function [UCG 4.1.3]. GDMT tables specify hold enalapril if creatinine rises >30% or if K ≥5.5 [GDMT Tables].
Beta blocker (carvedilol): UCG cautions beta blockers when SBP <90 [UCG 4.1.3]. GDMT tables say do not uptitrate carvedilol if volume overloaded and requires SBP >90 mmHg for titration [GDMT Tables].
Spironolactone: requires eGFR >30 and K <5.0 to use safely; hold if K ≥5.5 [GDMT Tables]. (Rising creatinine means you must check K/eGFR before continuing/adjusting.)
Diuretic (furosemide): if she is “cold” with AKI, aggressive diuresis can worsen renal perfusion (general clinical principle). If she is congested, diuresis may still be needed—but it must be guided by exam/vitals and senior review.

Escalation (before changing any HF medicines): treat as urgent decompensation / possible cardiogenic shock and get senior/physician review and higher level of care. HF Algorithm lists cardiogenic shock as a red flag requiring urgent attention [HF Algorithm].
And explicitly: any medication change (start/stop/hold/dose change, including holding enalapril/carvedilol or adjusting diuretics) requires cardiologist confirmation before administration.

Single next step right now: clarify whether she is hypotensive with hypoperfusion due to cardiogenic shock vs hypovolaemia/over-diuresis vs sepsis/other trigger, because that determines whether you’d hold GDMT and what acute therapy is safest.
