face

A colleague suggests increasing carvedilol and enalapril because the patient has HFrEF. Is that safe with this BP and creatinine trend?

smart_toy

With SBP 82 mmHg, dizziness, cool peripheries, and a rising creatinine, it is not safe to up‑titrate enalapril or carvedilol right now. Both UCG and the GDMT tables explicitly require caution/holding in this situation: UCG warns to use ACE inhibitors and beta blockers with caution when SBP <90 mmHg and to monitor renal function [UCG 4.1.3], and the GDMT tables list SBP >90 mmHg as a hemodynamic requirement for ACEi and carvedilol titration, with ACEi hold criteria including symptomatic hypotension and creatinine rise >30% [GDMT Tables]. Beta blockers should also not be up‑titrated when decompensated/volume overloaded [GDMT Tables]; the HF Algorithm likewise frames beta blockers as for when the patient is euvolemic/stable [HF Algorithm].

Medication safety issues to flag before any changes

Hemodynamic instability/possible shock: SBP 82/48 with cool peripheries → treat as a red flag requiring urgent escalation, not GDMT up‑titration [HF Algorithm].
Worsening renal function (AKI): heart failure/shock are UCG-listed causes of AKI; rising creatinine makes ACEi/MRA/diuretic changes higher risk without close monitoring [UCG 7.1.1].
RAAS blockade + MRA increases risk of hyperkalaemia/renal worsening: spironolactone requires K/Cr monitoring and has hold criteria at K ≥5.5 [GDMT Tables]. (Mechanism is general clinical knowledge; the monitoring/hold thresholds are from the table.)
Do not stop beta blocker abruptly is a general clinical safety principle; however, up‑titration is not appropriate in this unstable state [GDMT Tables].

Escalation step (before changing HF medicines):
Treat this as suspected cardiogenic shock/poor perfusion and escalate immediately to the most senior clinician/physician and arrange urgent transfer to a facility that can provide monitored resuscitation, inotropes/ICU-level care if needed (the HF Algorithm lists cardiogenic shock as a red flag) [HF Algorithm]. For the renal component, UCG advises AKI management can start at hospital level but the patient should be referred to higher level for more appropriate management when kidney function is worsening [UCG 7.1.1].

Because any change to enalapril/carvedilol/spironolactone/furosemide is a medication change, this requires cardiologist confirmation before administration (if no onsite cardiologist, obtain confirmation by phone via the receiving/referral team).

To determine whether she is “cold and wet” (needs urgent HF/shock pathway) vs “cold and dry” (possibly over-diuresed) before you call for medication instructions: what is her current SpO₂, respiratory exam (crepitations/orthopnoea), and urine output in the last 1–2 hours (or bladder volume if you can scan/catheterize)?
