face

A colleague suggests increasing carvedilol and enalapril because the patient has HFrEF. Is that safe with this BP and creatinine trend?

smart_toy

With BP 84/50 + dizziness + cool peripheries + rising creatinine, it is not safe to up‑titrate carvedilol or enalapril now. This constellation is a red flag for cardiogenic shock/poor perfusion and needs urgent escalation, not GDMT intensification [HF Algorithm]. Also, UCG explicitly cautions using ACE inhibitors and beta blockers when SBP <90 mmHg and to monitor renal function [UCG 4.1.3]. The GDMT tables also require SBP >90 mmHg for ACEi titration and carvedilol titration (and advise holding ACEi if Cr rises >30% from baseline) [GDMT Tables].

Single next step (before any HF medicine changes): treat this as an emergency—activate urgent referral/transfer to hospital/RR and get a senior clinician/doctor to review immediately (possible cardiogenic shock) [HF Algorithm].

Medication safety issues to keep in mind while you escalate (no changes yet):

Enalapril (ACEi): hypotension (SBP <90) and a creatinine rise are warning signs—GDMT tables say hold/avoid with symptomatic hypotension and hold if Cr ↑ >30% [GDMT Tables]; UCG advises caution when SBP <90 [UCG 4.1.3].
Carvedilol (beta blocker): GDMT tables require SBP >90 for titration and say do not up‑titrate if volume overloaded/decompensated [GDMT Tables]; UCG similarly cautions beta blockers when SBP <90 [UCG 4.1.3].
Spironolactone (MRA): needs potassium/renal monitoring; GDMT tables specify eGFR >30 and K <5.0 and close K/Cr monitoring, with hold if K ≥5.5 [GDMT Tables]. With rising creatinine, this becomes a key safety concern.
Furosemide: may be needed if she is congested, but in hypotension/AKI you’ll want senior clinician guidance and monitoring; any dose change requires cardiologist (or receiving physician) confirmation before administration.

Explicit rule for your setting: because any start/stop/hold/dose change is a medication change, this requires cardiologist/receiving-physician confirmation before administration (including “holding” enalapril/carvedilol or adjusting furosemide).
