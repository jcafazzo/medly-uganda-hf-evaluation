First, confirm she is truly stable today before any “ongoing/chronic HF” counselling: what are her current vitals (BP, HR, SpO₂, RR) and creatinine/potassium results today? UCG cautions ACE inhibitors and beta blockers when SBP <90 mmHg and says to monitor renal function [UCG 4.1.3]. Any medication dose change (increase/decrease/hold/extra dose) requires cardiologist confirmation before administration.

If she is stable enough for counselling, the key messages for her (patient-friendly) are:

Lifestyle priorities

Salt + fluids: Reduce salt intake and limit fluids to ~1–1.5 L/day if advised (this helps reduce swelling and breathlessness) [UCG 4.1.3]. [HF Algorithm]
Alcohol: Limit alcohol intake [UCG 4.1.3] [HF Algorithm]; PocketDoktor advises avoiding alcohol because it can worsen HF [PocketDoktor].
Activity: Regular exercise within symptom limits—avoid overexertion; rest when very symptomatic [UCG 4.1.3] [HF Algorithm].
Self-monitoring / when to seek help: Daily weight if possible; seek medical review if weight rises ≥1 kg overnight or 2–3 kg in a week, or if breathlessness at rest/while seated, worsening swelling, dizziness/fainting, or chest pain [PocketDoktor]. (These symptoms map to HF worsening and need review [HF Algorithm].)

What each medicine is for (simple explanation)

Furosemide (Lasix): a “water pill” that helps kidneys remove extra salt/water to reduce leg swelling and lung congestion; expect periodic blood tests for kidney function/electrolytes [PocketDoktor]. HF Algorithm uses a loop diuretic when the patient is hypervolemic (oedema, pulmonary congestion) [HF Algorithm].
Enalapril (ACE inhibitor): opens blood vessels and reduces salt/water retention, making it easier for the heart to pump; watch for low BP symptoms and possible dry cough [PocketDoktor]. In UCG, start low and increase gradually if tolerated, but use with caution if SBP <90 and monitor renal function [UCG 4.1.3]. (Dose specifics are in HF Algorithm tables if needed [HF Algorithm].)
Carvedilol (beta blocker): blocks adrenaline so the heart works less hard and beats more slowly; do not stop suddenly unless told by a doctor; watch for low BP/slow pulse symptoms [PocketDoktor]. HF Algorithm notes beta blockers are started/titrated when the patient is euvolemic (not acutely decompensated) [HF Algorithm].
Spironolactone: blocks harmful stress hormones and helps reduce scarring/stiffness; she will need ongoing blood tests for kidney function and potassium [PocketDoktor]. UCG includes spironolactone as an additional medicine in HF management [UCG 4.1.3].

Single next step for you in clinic: before reinforcing these messages and especially before discussing any “extra diuretic” or dose changes, confirm stability and baseline monitoring by recording weight, BP, HR, RR, SpO₂ (UCG recommends periodic monitoring of these in chronic HF) [UCG 4.1.3] and review renal function/electrolytes if available [HF Algorithm].
