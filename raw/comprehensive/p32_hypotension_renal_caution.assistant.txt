A colleague suggests increasing carvedilol and enalapril because the patient has HFrEF. Is that safe with this BP and creatinine trend?

smart_toy

With SBP 88 mmHg + symptoms (dizziness) + cool peripheries and a rise in creatinine, it is not safe to up‑titrate enalapril or carvedilol right now. Both UCG and the GDMT tables explicitly caution against ACE inhibitors and beta blockers when SBP <90 mmHg, and the GDMT tables add that ACEi should be held if creatinine rises >30% and that beta blockers should not be uptitrated when decompensated/volume overloaded [UCG 4.1.3; GDMT Tables]. In this situation, think shock/poor perfusion rather than “needs higher GDMT dose.”

Single next step: treat this as an unstable patient and escalate immediately to the on‑duty physician/medical officer for urgent assessment and referral/transfer planning (possible cardiogenic shock/AKI). This is urgent because UCG shock features include cold extremities, low BP, and reduced urine output [UCG 1.1.2].

Medication safety flags before any HF medicine changes (do not change until authorized):

Enalapril (ACEi): use with caution when SBP <90 and monitor renal function; GDMT: hold if symptomatic hypotension or Cr ↑ >30% and if K ≥5.5 [UCG 4.1.3; GDMT Tables].
Carvedilol (beta blocker): GDMT hemodynamic requirement SBP >90 and HR >55; do not uptitrate if decompensated/volume overloaded [GDMT Tables]. UCG also cautions beta blockers if SBP <90 [UCG 4.1.3].
Spironolactone: only if eGFR >30 and K <5.0; hold if K ≥5.5 with close K/Cr monitoring schedule [GDMT Tables].
Furosemide: can worsen hypotension/AKI if he is underfilled, but may be needed if congested—this balance needs prescriber decision with exam/labs.

Any action to hold, reduce, increase, stop, or add any of these medicines requires cardiologist/physician confirmation before administration.
