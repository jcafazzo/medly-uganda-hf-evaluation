face

A colleague suggests increasing carvedilol and enalapril because the patient has HFrEF. Is that safe with this BP and creatinine trend?

smart_toy

With SBP 89 mmHg + dizziness + cool peripheries + rising creatinine, this patient is not in a “stable chronic HF” titration state—she has features of poor perfusion/shock physiology and possible acute kidney injury from compromised renal perfusion. In this context, up‑titrating enalapril or carvedilol is not safe; both UCG and the GDMT tables specify SBP >90 mmHg for ACE‑i/beta‑blocker up‑titration, and UCG cautions ACE‑i and beta blockers when SBP <90 with renal monitoring; GDMT tables add “hold” triggers like symptomatic hypotension and creatinine rise >30% for enalapril [UCG 4.1.3; GDMT Tables]. The HF Algorithm also flags cardiogenic shock (hypotension, poor perfusion) as a red flag requiring urgent attention rather than routine GDMT escalation [HF Algorithm].

Single next step: treat this as a shock/AKI emergency and immediately escalate to the senior clinician/receiving hospital team for urgent transfer, while you do a rapid perfusion assessment (mental status, capillary refill, pulse quality/HR, RR/SpO₂, and urine output) to document severity and guide immediate stabilization [UCG 1.1.2; UCG 7.1.1; HF Algorithm].

Any change to HF medicines (including holding, reducing, or increasing enalapril/carvedilol/spironolactone/diuretics) requires cardiologist/receiving-doctor confirmation before administration.

To decide how urgent the transfer is and what key data to hand over: what are her heart rate/rhythm (ECG if available) and SpO₂/respiratory status right now (any pulmonary oedema signs ongoing)?
