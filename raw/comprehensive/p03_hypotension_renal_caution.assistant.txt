face

A colleague suggests increasing carvedilol and enalapril because the patient has HFrEF. Is that safe with this BP and creatinine trend?

smart_toy

No—up‑titrating carvedilol or enalapril is not safe in this moment with SBP 84 mmHg and worsening renal function. Both the Uganda Clinical Guidelines and the GDMT tables require SBP >90 mmHg for ACE inhibitor and beta‑blocker titration, and UCG specifically cautions to use ACE inhibitors and beta blockers with caution when SBP <90 and to monitor renal function [UCG 4.1.3], [GDMT Tables]. For enalapril specifically, the GDMT tables also advise holding/avoiding dose increases if creatinine rises >30% or there is symptomatic hypotension [GDMT Tables].

Single next step (now): Treat this as possible cardiogenic shock/poor perfusion and escalate immediately to the medical officer/receiving hospital for urgent transfer while stabilizing—do not change HF medicine doses until a doctor/cardiologist reviews and authorizes. Any dose change (increase, decrease, hold, or extra dose) requires cardiologist confirmation before administration. This includes carvedilol, enalapril, spironolactone, and furosemide.

Medication safety issues to highlight to the doctor/cardiologist before any changes

Enalapril (ACEi): titration requires SBP >90 and Cr/K monitoring; hold/avoid titration if symptomatic hypotension or Cr ↑ >30% [GDMT Tables]. UCG also cautions ACEi use when SBP <90 [UCG 4.1.3].
Carvedilol (beta blocker): titration requires SBP >90 (and HR >55); and do not uptitrate if decompensated/volume overloaded [GDMT Tables]. UCG similarly says add beta blocker only “when stable” and cautions use when SBP <90 [UCG 4.1.3].
Spironolactone (MRA): requires eGFR >30 and K <5.0 to use safely; hold if K ≥5.5 and follow close K/Cr monitoring [GDMT Tables]. With rising creatinine and unknown K, this is a key risk area.
Furosemide: If she is oliguric/AKI in the setting of HF/shock, UCG notes IV furosemide may be used according to response (sometimes high doses), but this should be directed by the prescriber during stabilization/referral [UCG 7.1.1]. (Again: any dose change requires cardiologist/doctor confirmation before administration.)

One critical question (to decide immediate stabilization priorities before transfer):
What is her current SpO₂ and respiratory status right now (on room air or oxygen), and is she passing urine (rough estimate in the last 6 hours)? This helps determine whether she’s primarily “cold and wet” with ongoing pulmonary oedema versus predominant shock/AKI.
