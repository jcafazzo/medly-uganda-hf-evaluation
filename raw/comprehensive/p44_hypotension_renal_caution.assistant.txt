Because you asked about HF medication safety “before changing medicines”: the key immediate safety point is that symptomatic hypotension (SBP <90) and rising creatinine are hold/avoid flags for RAAS blockers and for uptitration of beta blockers:

Carvedilol/beta blocker: do not uptitrate if decompensated/volume overloaded; requires SBP >90 and HR >55 for titration [GDMT Tables]. (Also, as a general clinical principle, abrupt stopping can be harmful—so decisions to hold need senior review.)
Spironolactone/MRA: hold if K ≥5.5; requires adequate renal function (eGFR >30, K<5.0 for safe use) and close K/Cr monitoring [GDMT Tables].
Diuretics: if oliguria/fluid retention is present, UCG allows furosemide according to response (high doses may be necessary) in acute renal failure, but this must be balanced against hypotension and perfusion concerns and should be physician-led [UCG 7.1.1].

Medication-change safety rule: any holding, starting, stopping, or dose change (including “holding enalapril today” or “extra furosemide”) requires cardiologist/physician confirmation before administration.

A colleague suggests increasing carvedilol and enalapril because the patient has HFrEF. Is that safe with this BP and creatinine trend?

smart_toy

With SBP 89 mmHg plus dizziness and rising creatinine, it is not safe to uptitrate either enalapril or carvedilol right now. Both UCG and the GDMT titration tables flag SBP >90 mmHg as a requirement for ACE‑I and carvedilol titration, and UCG specifically cautions ACE‑I and beta blockers when SBP <90 mmHg with renal monitoring [UCG 4.1.3], [GDMT Tables]. For enalapril specifically, the GDMT tables advise hold/avoid if creatinine rises >30% or if symptomatic hypotension is present [GDMT Tables].

Single next step: treat this as hemodynamic instability/possible cardiogenic shock and immediately escalate to a senior clinician/receiving hospital team for urgent review and referral, before making any HF medication changes [HF Algorithm]. Any dose increase, decrease, or holding of enalapril/carvedilol/spironolactone/furosemide requires cardiologist/doctor confirmation before administration.

To decide what the physician is most likely to do (and what to communicate in the referral): what is her current heart rate and SpO₂, and does she have signs of congestion right now (basal crepitations/JVP raised/leg oedema) or is she “dry”? This determines whether hypotension is more consistent with pump failure vs over-diuresis/volume depletion, and it affects whether diuretics/vasodilators/beta blocker should be held vs continued pending doctor confirmation.
