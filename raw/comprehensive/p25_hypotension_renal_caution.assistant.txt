face

A colleague suggests increasing carvedilol and enalapril because the patient has HFrEF. Is that safe with this BP and creatinine trend?

smart_toy

With SBP 82 mmHg + dizziness + cool peripheries + rising creatinine, this patient is not in a safe hemodynamic state for GDMT up‑titration. Both UCG and the GDMT tables flag SBP <90 mmHg / symptomatic hypotension and renal deterioration as caution/hold conditions for ACE‑I and for beta blockers (at least “do not uptitrate”) [UCG 4.1.3]; [GDMT Tables].

Single next step (before changing any HF medicines): treat this as possible cardiogenic shock/poor perfusion and immediately escalate to the most senior clinician available and activate urgent referral/transfer (this is beyond routine clinic titration) [HF Algorithm] (red flags); also consistent with the need for urgent management of shock features per general clinical principle.

Medication safety issues to highlight (for your discussion with the doctor/cardiologist):

Enalapril (ACE‑I): UCG cautions ACE‑I use when SBP <90 and to monitor renal function [UCG 4.1.3]. The GDMT tables advise hold/avoid if symptomatic hypotension or if creatinine rises >30% and to only titrate when SBP >90 [GDMT Tables].
Carvedilol (beta blocker): UCG cautions beta blockers when SBP <90 [UCG 4.1.3]. GDMT tables require SBP >90 and HR >55 for titration and say do not uptitrate if decompensated/volume overloaded [GDMT Tables].
Spironolactone (MRA): requires K <5.0 and eGFR >30; hold if K ≥5.5 and monitor K/Cr closely [GDMT Tables]. With rising creatinine, hyperkalaemia risk is a key concern (general clinical principle).
Furosemide: in renal impairment with fluid retention/oliguria, UCG allows furosemide according to response (high doses may be necessary) [UCG 7.1.2]. But in hypotension/shock, diuresis can worsen perfusion if the patient is not congested (general clinical principle).

Critical rule for your setting: Any start/stop/hold/increase/decrease (including temporarily holding ACE‑I/beta blocker/MRA or giving additional diuretic) requires cardiologist confirmation before administration. If no onsite cardiologist, obtain phone confirmation from the cardiologist/most senior physician before implementing the change.

Focused question to guide safe escalation/treatment choice:
Right now, is he still congested (e.g., basal crepitations, raised JVP, peripheral oedema) or is he dry—and what are his HR, SpO₂, urine output, and potassium with the new creatinine?
