face

A colleague suggests increasing carvedilol and enalapril because the patient has HFrEF. Is that safe with this BP and creatinine trend?

smart_toy

With SBP 88 mmHg + dizziness + cool peripheries + rising creatinine, it is not safe to up‑titrate enalapril or carvedilol now. These are red flags for hemodynamic instability/poor perfusion (possible cardiogenic shock physiology) and possible AKI, where the priority is urgent escalation—not GDMT intensification. The UCG specifically says use ACE inhibitors and beta blockers with caution if SBP <90 mmHg and monitor renal function [UCG 4.1.3], and the GDMT tables advise holding enalapril if creatinine rises >30% (and if K ≥5.5) and holding ACEi for symptomatic hypotension [GDMT Tables]. For carvedilol, titration requires SBP >90 and you should not up‑titrate if volume overloaded [GDMT Tables].

Single next step: Escalate immediately for urgent physician/cardiologist review and arrange transfer/admission (manage as “cold”/poor perfusion with worsening renal function), before making any HF medication changes. This is consistent with HF red flags (hypotension/poor perfusion) requiring urgent attention and referral [HF Algorithm], and UCG guidance on acute renal failure where worsening kidney function should prompt referral for higher-level management [UCG 7.1.1].

Medication safety issues to flag (before any changes):

Up‑titrating enalapril now is unsafe in symptomatic hypotension and rising creatinine; ACEi require close renal/K monitoring and are cautioned when SBP <90 [UCG 4.1.3], with hold criteria in the GDMT tables [GDMT Tables].
Up‑titrating carvedilol now is unsafe because GDMT titration requires SBP >90 and stability; also avoid uptitration if decompensated/volume overloaded [GDMT Tables].
Spironolactone requires K/Cr monitoring and eGFR >30; if renal function is worsening, hyperkalaemia risk rises—so do not change dose without labs/physician decision [GDMT Tables].
Loop diuretic (furosemide): if he is hypoperfused/AKI, diuretic decisions need careful assessment of congestion vs dehydration and monitoring—again, physician-led.

Cardiologist confirmation rule (important): Any holding, stopping, starting, or dose change of enalapril/carvedilol/spironolactone/furosemide requires cardiologist (or designated physician) confirmation before administration.

To guide the urgency and what the receiving team needs, one key piece is missing:

What is Joseph’s current SpO₂ and lung status right now—does he have basal crepitations/raised JVP/leg oedema (congested) or is his chest relatively clear (possibly over-diuresed/dehydrated)?
