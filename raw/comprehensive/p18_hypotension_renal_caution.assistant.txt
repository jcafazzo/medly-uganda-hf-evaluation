smart_toy

With SBP 84/50 + cool peripheries + rising creatinine, it is not safe to up‑titrate enalapril or carvedilol right now—this is a red‑flag low-perfusion state (possible cardiogenic shock/acute decompensation) and the guidelines explicitly caution against ACEi/beta blocker use when SBP <90 and emphasize renal monitoring [UCG 4.1.3]. The GDMT titration tables also require SBP >90 mmHg for enalapril titration and carvedilol uptitration, and advise holding ACEi if Cr rises >30% or symptomatic hypotension occurs [GDMT Tables].

Single next step: treat this as an emergency and escalate immediately for physician/senior clinician-led management and urgent transfer, while you start/continue high‑concentration oxygen targeting SpO₂ >95% and keep her propped up if pulmonary oedema is present [UCG 4.1.4].

On medication safety issues before any HF medicine changes:

Up‑titrating carvedilol is unsafe in decompensation/possible shock; carvedilol uptitration requires SBP >90 and you should not uptitrate if volume overloaded [GDMT Tables].
Up‑titrating enalapril is unsafe at SBP 84/50; enalapril titration requires SBP >90, and it should be held if Cr ↑ >30% or K ≥5.5 [GDMT Tables]. UCG also warns to use ACEi/beta blockers with caution when SBP <90 and to monitor renal function [UCG 4.1.3].
Spironolactone safety: ensure K/renal function thresholds are acceptable (hold if K ≥5.5) and monitor closely [GDMT Tables].
Any dose increase, decrease, or holding of enalapril/carvedilol/spironolactone/furosemide is a medication change and requires cardiologist confirmation before administration—in this unstable state, that confirmation should come from the receiving physician/cardiology team (or the on-call medical officer) before you alter chronic HF medicines.
