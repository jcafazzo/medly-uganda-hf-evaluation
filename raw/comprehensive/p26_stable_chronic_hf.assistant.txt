While you verify that, here is a patient-friendly explanation you can use in clinic:

Medicines (plain language)
Furosemide (Lasix) – “water pill”: helps the kidneys remove extra salt and water, so swelling reduces and breathing improves [PocketDoktor]. It is used when someone is “fluid overloaded” (swollen legs, lung congestion) [HF Algorithm]. Expect periodic blood tests for salts and kidney function [PocketDoktor].
Enalapril (ACE inhibitor): opens blood vessels and reduces salt/water retention, making it easier for the heart to pump [PocketDoktor]. In Uganda guidance it’s started low and increased gradually if tolerated [UCG 4.1.3] (dose changes still need cardiologist confirmation).
Carvedilol (beta blocker): blocks adrenaline so the heart beats more slowly and with less strain [PocketDoktor]. It is added when stable and increased gradually [UCG 4.1.3; HF Algorithm]. Important message: do not stop suddenly unless a doctor tells you to [PocketDoktor].
Spironolactone (MRA): blocks stress hormones that contribute to scarring/stiffness of the heart; requires ongoing bloodwork for kidney function and potassium [PocketDoktor]. It is listed as an additional medicine for chronic HF in UCG [UCG 4.1.3].
Lifestyle and self-care that matter most
Salt: reduce salt—avoid adding salt when cooking and limit packaged/processed foods [UCG 4.1.3; PocketDoktor].
Fluids: some patients are advised to limit fluids; UCG suggests 1–1.5 L/day in HF management (individualize based on clinician plan) [UCG 4.1.3; PocketDoktor].
Alcohol: limit or avoid alcohol; PocketDoktor encourages stopping alcohol because it can worsen HF [PocketDoktor]. UCG also says limit alcohol [UCG 4.1.3].
Activity/exercise: do regular exercise within symptom limits—keep active but avoid overexertion [UCG 4.1.3; PocketDoktor].
Home monitoring / when to seek help:
If she can weigh: see a clinician if +1 kg overnight or +2–3 kg in a week [PocketDoktor].
Use the traffic-light symptoms: seek help for breathlessness at rest/while seated, rapid weight gain, worsening swelling, fainting, or severe shortness of breath/dizziness [PocketDoktor].

You can find all these details in the Medications section of the Pocket Doktor.
