A colleague suggests increasing carvedilol and enalapril because the patient has HFrEF. Is that safe with this BP and creatinine trend?

smart_toy

With SBP 88 mmHg + dizziness/cool peripheries and rising creatinine, up‑titrating enalapril or carvedilol is not safe right now. Both UCG and the titration tables require SBP >90 mmHg for ACEi/beta-blocker titration, and UCG explicitly cautions using ACE inhibitors and beta blockers when SBP <90 with renal monitoring [UCG 4.1.3; GDMT Tables]. The GDMT tables also specify to hold ACEi if symptomatic hypotension or if creatinine rises >30% from baseline [GDMT Tables], and do not uptitrate carvedilol if volume overloaded [GDMT Tables]. HF Algorithm further emphasizes beta blockers are for when the patient is euvolemic (not acutely decompensated) [HF Algorithm].

Medication safety issues to flag (before any changes):

Enalapril (ACEi): hypotension + renal function worsening → meets “caution/hold” conditions in tables (symptomatic hypotension; Cr rise threshold) [UCG 4.1.3; GDMT Tables].
Carvedilol (beta blocker): do not uptitrate in decompensation/volume overload and needs SBP >90 for uptitration [GDMT Tables]; use in instability needs senior review [UCG 4.1.3].
Spironolactone (MRA): requires renal function and potassium safety thresholds (eGFR >30, K <5.0 to use; hold if K ≥5.5) and close K/Cr monitoring schedule [GDMT Tables]—rising creatinine increases risk.
Furosemide: if hypotensive with renal dysfunction, diuretic strategy depends on whether he is congested vs intravascularly depleted; this is a high-risk balancing decision and should be senior-led.

Escalation step to take now (single next step):
Treat this as possible cardiogenic shock/poor perfusion + AKI and escalate immediately to the most senior clinician available and arrange urgent transfer/admission for monitoring and management (BP/SpO₂, urine output, renal function/electrolytes) [UCG 7.1.1].

Any action to increase, reduce, or hold enalapril/carvedilol/spironolactone/furosemide is a medication change and requires cardiologist confirmation before administration.
