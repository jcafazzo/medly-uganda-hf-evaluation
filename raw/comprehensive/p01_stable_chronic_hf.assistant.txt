Confirm she is clinically stable today by checking BP, pulse, SpO₂, weight, and asking about orthopnoea/PND and recent weight gain [HF Algorithm; UCG 4.1.3]. This ensures it’s safe to give routine counselling and to avoid advising changes when she’s decompensated.

While you check those, for the counselling content the patient asked for, here is the core message (no dose changes implied):

Furosemide (“water pill”) helps the kidneys remove extra salt and water to reduce swelling and breathlessness; she should expect periodic blood tests to monitor kidney function/electrolytes [PocketDoktor].
ACE inhibitor (e.g., enalapril) opens blood vessels and reduces salt/water retention so the heart pumps more easily; watch for symptoms of low BP and (sometimes) a dry cough [PocketDoktor].
Beta blocker (e.g., carvedilol) blocks adrenaline so the heart doesn’t work as hard; watch for dizziness/slow pulse, and do not stop suddenly unless a doctor instructs [PocketDoktor].
Spironolactone blocks stress hormones that cause scarring/stiffness; ongoing kidney function and potassium checks are expected [PocketDoktor].
Salt + fluids: reduce salt intake and limit fluids to ~1–1.5 L/day if advised (especially if she tends to retain fluid) [UCG 4.1.3; PocketDoktor].
Alcohol + activity: limit alcohol; do regular exercise within symptom limits (avoid overexertion) [UCG 4.1.3; HF Algorithm].
Home monitoring: monitor weight (if possible) and symptoms; seek care if weight rises rapidly (≥1 kg overnight or 2–3 kg/week) or if symptoms worsen [PocketDoktor; HF Algorithm].
