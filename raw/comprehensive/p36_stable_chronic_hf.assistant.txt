While you check that, here is patient-friendly wording you can use to answer her question (for when she is stable enough to talk):

Salt (most important lifestyle change)

“Salt makes your body hold onto water. When you eat salty foods or add salt, fluid can collect in your lungs and legs and make breathing worse.”
Advise low-salt diet and avoid adding salt/packaged foods [PocketDoktor], and UCG recommends reducing salt intake [UCG 4.1.3].

Fluids

“If you drink too much, the extra fluid can build up and worsen heart failure.”
Many patients are advised to limit fluids to about 1–1.5 L/day (your clinician will individualize this) [UCG 4.1.3], [PocketDoktor].

Alcohol

“Alcohol can weaken the heart muscle and worsen heart failure—best is to avoid it.” [PocketDoktor] (UCG also advises limiting alcohol) [UCG 4.1.3].

Activity

“Regular gentle exercise helps your heart and your body stay strong, but don’t overexert—stop and rest if symptoms start.” [UCG 4.1.3], [PocketDoktor].

Medicines (what each one is for)

Furosemide (Lasix) – the ‘water pill’
“This helps your kidneys remove extra salt and water, so swelling reduces and breathing improves.” [PocketDoktor].
Tell her: “We will sometimes do blood tests while you’re on it.” [PocketDoktor].
ACE inhibitor (e.g., enalapril)
“This relaxes blood vessels and helps the body hold less salt and water, so the heart pumps more easily.” [PocketDoktor].
“If you feel symptoms of low blood pressure (dizziness), report it. A dry cough can happen with ACE inhibitors.” [PocketDoktor].
Beta blocker (e.g., carvedilol)
“This blocks stress hormones so your heart doesn’t have to work as hard or beat as fast.” [PocketDoktor].
Key safety message: “Do not stop it suddenly unless your doctor tells you to.” [PocketDoktor].
Spironolactone
“This blocks hormones that make the heart stiff and scarred; it helps protect the heart over time.” [PocketDoktor].
“We need ongoing blood tests for kidney function and potassium while you use it.” [PocketDoktor].
