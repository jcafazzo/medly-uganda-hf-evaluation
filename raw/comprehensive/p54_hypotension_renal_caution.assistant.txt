face

A colleague suggests increasing carvedilol and enalapril because the patient has HFrEF. Is that safe with this BP and creatinine trend?

smart_toy

With SBP 89 mmHg, dizziness, cool peripheries and a rising creatinine, it is not safe to up‑titrate enalapril or carvedilol now; this presentation fits a red-flag/unstable state where the priority is urgent escalation and stabilization, not GDMT intensification [UCG 4.1.3], [HF Algorithm].

Medication safety issues to flag before any HF medicine change

ACE inhibitor (enalapril): UCG specifically cautions to use ACE inhibitors with caution if SBP <90 mmHg and to monitor renal function [UCG 4.1.3]. The GDMT tables also require SBP >90 mmHg for titration and advise holding if Cr rises >30% (and to check Cr/K 1–2 weeks after changes) [GDMT Tables].
Beta blocker (carvedilol): UCG again cautions with SBP <90 mmHg [UCG 4.1.3]. GDMT tables: carvedilol uptitration requires SBP >90 and HR >55, and you should not uptitrate if volume overloaded/decompensated [GDMT Tables].
Spironolactone: needs close K/Cr monitoring; hold if K ≥5.5 and requires adequate renal function (eGFR >30) [GDMT Tables]. Rising creatinine makes this a key safety concern.
Furosemide: may be needed if she is congested, but with hypotension/AKI you need careful assessment of volume status and monitoring; UCG notes furosemide use in HF and also in fluid retention/oliguria in renal failure, but dosing decisions should be made with senior support and monitoring [UCG 4.1.3], [UCG 7.1.2].

Escalation step to take before changing any HF medicines (next step):
Treat this as a potential cardiogenic shock/poor perfusion picture and escalate immediately to the most senior clinician available / arrange urgent referral/admission while you reassess ABCs and perfusion [HF Algorithm] (red flags list includes cardiogenic shock with hypotension and poor perfusion).

Any medication change (including increasing, holding, or reducing enalapril/carvedilol/spironolactone/diuretics) requires cardiologist (or physician) confirmation before administration.

To decide what to communicate to the receiving team and whether congestion is driving the AKI vs low filling (“over-diuresis”), what is her heart rate and SpO₂, and on exam does she look congested (raised JVP, crackles, edema) or dry (flat JVP, no crackles)?
