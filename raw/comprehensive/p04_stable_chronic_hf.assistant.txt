What matters most day-to-day (medicines + lifestyle)

1) Take medicines every day—especially the “heart-protecting” ones
For HFrEF/HFmrEF, the HF Algorithm recommends ongoing therapy with: a loop diuretic if congested, ACEi/ARB/ARNi, beta blocker, spironolactone, and also SGLT2 inhibitor for most patients [HF Algorithm]. UCG also emphasizes continued treatment with progressive dose increases when stable [UCG 4.1.3].
Any starting/stopping or dose change requires cardiologist confirmation before administration.

2) Salt + fluids

UCG recommends reducing salt intake and limiting fluids to 1–1.5 L/day for HF patients [UCG 4.1.3].
PocketDoktor reinforces: avoid adding salt, avoid packaged foods, and discuss the exact fluid amount with the clinician because too much fluid can worsen HF; some patients are advised <1.5 L/day [PocketDoktor].

3) Alcohol

UCG advises to limit alcohol intake in chronic HF [UCG 4.1.3].
PocketDoktor encourages stopping alcohol (and tobacco) because they can worsen HF [PocketDoktor].

4) Activity

UCG: regular exercise within symptom limits [UCG 4.1.3].
PocketDoktor: exercise helps, but the patient should not overexert and should discuss a safe exercise plan [PocketDoktor].
How to explain each HF medicine simply (and what the patient should watch for)

Furosemide (“water pill”)

Helps the kidneys remove extra water and salt, reducing swelling and breathlessness from fluid overload [PocketDoktor].
UCG uses furosemide as core therapy for congestion in HF [UCG 4.1.3].
What to watch: needing periodic blood tests for kidney function/electrolytes [PocketDoktor].
Any extra dose or dose increase/decrease requires cardiologist confirmation before administration.

ACE inhibitor (e.g., enalapril)

Helps open blood vessels and reduce salt/water retention so the heart pumps more easily [PocketDoktor].
UCG includes ACE inhibitors as foundational HF treatment and notes to start low and increase gradually if tolerated [UCG 4.1.3].
What to watch: symptoms of low BP; ACE‑I may cause dry cough [PocketDoktor]. UCG cautions using ACE‑I if SBP <90 and to monitor renal function [UCG 4.1.3].
Any dose change/holding requires cardiologist confirmation before administration.

Beta blocker (e.g., carvedilol)

Blocks adrenaline so the heart does not work as hard or beat too fast [PocketDoktor].
HF Algorithm: beta blockers are part of HFrEF/HFmrEF therapy and should be titrated toward a goal HR 60–80 bpm, started when the patient is euvolemic (not acutely decompensated) [HF Algorithm].
What to watch: low BP/slow pulse; do not stop suddenly unless a doctor instructs [PocketDoktor].
Any titration/holding requires cardiologist confirmation before administration.

Spironolactone

Blocks stress hormones that make the heart stiff and scarred [PocketDoktor].
HF Algorithm includes spironolactone as part of the HFrEF/HFmrEF package [HF Algorithm].
What to watch: need ongoing bloodwork for kidney function and potassium [PocketDoktor].
Any dose change/holding requires cardiologist confirmation before administration.
