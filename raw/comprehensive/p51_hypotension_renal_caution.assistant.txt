face

A colleague suggests increasing carvedilol and enalapril because the patient has HFrEF. Is that safe with this BP and creatinine trend?

smart_toy

With SBP 86 mmHg + dizziness + cool peripheries + rising creatinine, this is hemodynamic instability/poor perfusion (possible cardiogenic shock physiology). In that situation, up‑titrating carvedilol or enalapril is not safe—both UCG and the GDMT tables specify SBP >90 mmHg as a requirement for ACE‑I and carvedilol titration, and UCG specifically cautions ACE‑I and beta blockers when SBP <90 with renal monitoring. [UCG 4.1.3], [GDMT Tables]

Medication safety issues to flag (before any changes):

ACE‑I (enalapril): use with caution when SBP <90 and with rising creatinine; GDMT tables also advise holding ACE‑I if creatinine rises >30% from baseline or if symptomatic hypotension. [UCG 4.1.3], [GDMT Tables]
Beta blocker (carvedilol): do not up‑titrate when decompensated/volume overloaded, and requires SBP >90 to titrate. [GDMT Tables]
MRA (spironolactone): needs close K/Cr monitoring; GDMT tables advise holding if K ≥5.5 (and require eGFR >30, K <5.0 to use safely). [GDMT Tables]
Loop diuretic (furosemide): may be needed for congestion, but in shock/hypotension you must reassess perfusion/renal function carefully; high doses may be required in oliguria in renal failure per UCG renal guidance. [UCG 7.1.1], [HF Algorithm]

Escalation / level-of-care:

This patient meets red-flag criteria for urgent senior review and referral (hypotension with poor perfusion + AKI trend). Arrange urgent transfer to a higher level where vasopressors/inotropes and close monitoring are available if needed. (The HF Algorithm flags cardiogenic shock as urgent.) [HF Algorithm]

Single next step right now: Do NOT increase carvedilol or enalapril; instead urgently escalate to the responsible physician/cardiologist for shock-level review while arranging transfer. Any medication change (holding, reducing, increasing, or giving extra doses) requires cardiologist/physician confirmation before administration. [UCG 4.1.3], [GDMT Tables]

To help the physician decide quickly: what is her heart rate and rhythm (ECG if available) and what is her urine output/last void (oliguria vs normal)?
