face

A colleague suggests increasing carvedilol and enalapril because the patient has HFrEF. Is that safe with this BP and creatinine trend?

smart_toy

Not safe to up‑titrate either carvedilol or enalapril right now. With SBP 86 mmHg, dizziness, cool peripheries, and a creatinine rise, she meets red flags for poor perfusion/cardiogenic shock—this is an urgent escalation scenario, not a routine GDMT titration visit [HF Algorithm]. UCG also cautions that ACE inhibitors and beta blockers must be used with caution when SBP <90 mmHg and renal function should be monitored [UCG 4.1.3]. The GDMT tables specifically require SBP >90 mmHg for enalapril titration and list hold criteria for enalapril of symptomatic hypotension and creatinine rise >30% [GDMT Tables]. For carvedilol, the hemodynamic requirement includes SBP >90 mmHg, and you should not uptitrate if decompensated/volume overloaded [GDMT Tables].

Medication safety issues to flag before any HF medicine change

ACE inhibitor (enalapril): risk of worsening hypotension and kidney function in this context; per tables, hold/avoid uptitration with symptomatic hypotension or Cr ↑ >30% [GDMT Tables].
Beta blocker (carvedilol): do not uptitrate when decompensated; requires SBP >90 mmHg for uptitration [GDMT Tables].
Spironolactone: needs close K/Cr monitoring and should be held if K ≥5.5; in AKI risk this becomes a key safety concern [GDMT Tables].
Diuretics (furosemide): may be necessary if congested, but in hypotension/AKI risk, any escalation can worsen renal perfusion—needs senior oversight.

Critical safety rule: any start/stop/hold/increase/decrease of enalapril, carvedilol, spironolactone, or furosemide requires cardiologist/doctor confirmation before administration.

Single next step (do now, in clinic)

Assess and document perfusion/instability immediately: measure SpO₂, heart rate, respiratory rate, mental status, and look for ongoing pulmonary oedema signs; if SpO₂ <90%, start oxygen per hypoxaemia guidance [UCG 1.4]. This is to determine if she needs immediate resuscitation/ambulance transfer as suspected shock [HF Algorithm].

Focused question: What are her SpO₂ and heart rate right now, and do you have her current potassium and the % creatinine rise from baseline (or the two values)?
