face

A colleague suggests increasing carvedilol and enalapril because the patient has HFrEF. Is that safe with this BP and creatinine trend?

smart_toy

With SBP 89 mmHg + dizziness + cool peripheries + rising creatinine, this looks like poor perfusion/cardiogenic shock physiology and possible AKI due to compromised renal perfusion—so up‑titrating enalapril or carvedilol is not safe right now. The UCG specifically cautions that ACE inhibitors and beta blockers should be used with caution when SBP <90 mmHg and renal function must be monitored [UCG 4.1.3], and AKI can be caused by heart failure/shock [UCG 7.1.1]. The GDMT tables also set hemodynamic requirements/hold criteria (e.g., avoid/hold ACEi with symptomatic hypotension or creatinine rise >30%; carvedilol requires SBP >90 and avoid uptitration if decompensated) [GDMT Tables].

Single next step now (before changing any HF medicines): treat this as an emergency and escalate immediately to a senior doctor/receiving hospital (arrange urgent referral/ambulance) for shock + AKI management. This is the priority before any GDMT titration [UCG 7.1.1].

Medication safety issues to explicitly flag (so you can communicate them clearly during escalation):

Do NOT up‑titrate ACEi (enalapril) with SBP <90 or worsening renal function; GDMT tables advise holding with symptomatic hypotension or Cr rise >30% [GDMT Tables], and UCG cautions ACEi in SBP <90 with renal monitoring [UCG 4.1.3].
Do NOT up‑titrate beta blocker (carvedilol) in instability/decompensation; carvedilol titration requires SBP >90 and “do not uptitrate if volume overloaded” [GDMT Tables]; UCG cautions beta blockers when SBP <90 [UCG 4.1.3].
Spironolactone needs K/renal monitoring; GDMT tables require eGFR >30 and K <5.0 and recommend close K/Cr monitoring; hold if K ≥5.5 [GDMT Tables].
Any change (hold/stop/reduce/increase) to enalapril/carvedilol/spironolactone/furosemide is a medication change and requires cardiologist confirmation before administration (or, if no cardiologist onsite, confirmation by the responsible receiving physician/cardiology team before the change).

Focused question (needed to decide what the receiving team should prioritize and whether any meds must be urgently held): what are his current potassium (K) and the actual creatinine values (baseline and current), and is he currently volume overloaded on exam (JVP raised, lung crackles, peripheral oedema) or more dry (no oedema/JVP low)?
