face

A colleague suggests increasing carvedilol and enalapril because the patient has HFrEF. Is that safe with this BP and creatinine trend?

smart_toy

With SBP 89 mmHg + dizziness + cool peripheries + rising creatinine, it is not safe to up‑titrate enalapril or carvedilol right now. Both UCG and the GDMT tables flag SBP >90 mmHg as a requirement for titration, and UCG specifically says use ACE inhibitors and beta blockers with caution if SBP <90 mmHg and monitor renal function [UCG 4.1.3], [GDMT Tables]. The GDMT tables also note to hold ACEi/ARB/ARNI if creatinine rises >30% from baseline or if there is symptomatic hypotension [GDMT Tables]. For beta blockers (carvedilol), do not up‑titrate if decompensated/volume overloaded, and BP should be >90 mmHg [GDMT Tables].

Medication safety issues to flag before changing anything

Possible cardiogenic shock/low-output state: hypotension + cool peripheries + renal deterioration is a red-flag scenario requiring urgent escalation (this is not a routine GDMT titration situation) [HF Algorithm].
ACEi (enalapril): risk of worsening renal function/hypotension; titration requires SBP >90 and close Cr/K monitoring; hold if Cr rises >30% or symptomatic hypotension (per GDMT tables) [UCG 4.1.3], [GDMT Tables].
Beta blocker (carvedilol): do not up‑titrate in decompensation; SBP should be >90 and HR >55 for titration [GDMT Tables]. (Also, as a general clinical principle, beta blockers can worsen low-output states if increased during shock.)
Spironolactone: requires K and renal monitoring with clear hold thresholds (hold if K ≥5.5) [GDMT Tables].
Furosemide: if she is intravascularly depleted, aggressive diuresis can worsen hypotension/AKI; if she is congested, under-diuresis worsens perfusion/oxygenation—so volume assessment is critical (general clinical principle).

Escalation principle (before any med change): because any dose increase/decrease/holding is a medication change, this change requires cardiologist (or receiving senior physician) confirmation before administration.

Single next step now: urgently assess for shock severity and congestion at bedside to decide immediate escalation/transfer—specifically, check SpO₂ and respiratory distress, and examine for pulmonary oedema/JVP/peripheral edema as part of HF assessment [HF Algorithm].
