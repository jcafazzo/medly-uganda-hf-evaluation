How to explain her key HF medicines (in plain language)
Furosemide (Lasix) – “water pill”: helps the kidneys pass out extra salt and water, so swelling and breathlessness reduce. She should expect periodic blood tests because diuretics can affect salts and kidney function [PocketDoktor]. Loop diuretics are used when there are signs of fluid overload (e.g., oedema) [HF Algorithm].
Enalapril (ACE inhibitor): opens blood vessels and reduces salt/water retention, making it easier for the heart to pump [PocketDoktor]. In UCG, ACE inhibitors are started low and increased gradually if tolerated [UCG 4.1.3]. (Remind her to report dizziness/low BP symptoms) [PocketDoktor].
Carvedilol (beta blocker): blocks “adrenaline,” so the heart beats more calmly and does less work; it helps the heart over time. A key safety point: do not stop suddenly unless a doctor tells her to [PocketDoktor]. In HFrEF, beta blockers are part of standard therapy and are titrated toward a goal resting HR about 60–80 bpm [HF Algorithm].
Spironolactone (aldosterone antagonist/MRA): blocks stress hormones that cause scarring/stiffness of the heart; it improves long‑term outcomes. She should expect blood tests for kidney function and potassium monitoring [PocketDoktor; HF Algorithm].
Lifestyle changes that matter most (Uganda guideline + PocketDoktor)
Salt: reduce salt intake (avoid adding salt; avoid packaged/salty foods). This helps reduce fluid retention and swelling [UCG 4.1.3; PocketDoktor].
Fluids: some patients are advised to limit fluids; UCG suggests about 1–1.5 L/day in HF management [UCG 4.1.3]. Whether she needs strict restriction depends on her congestion status—confirm with the clinician plan.
Alcohol and smoking: minimize/avoid alcohol and stop smoking; alcohol can worsen HF and overall health [PocketDoktor; UCG 4.1.3].
Activity/exercise: do regular exercise within the limits of her symptoms—avoid overexertion, but staying active is beneficial [UCG 4.1.3; PocketDoktor].
