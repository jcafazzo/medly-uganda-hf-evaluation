For John with chronic HFrEF (EF 35%), the key “ongoing” message is: medicines + diet/fluids + daily symptom/weight monitoring work together to keep fluid down, reduce the heart’s workload, and prevent worsening [HF Algorithm; PocketDoktor; UCG 4.1.3].

While you get those vitals, here is a patient-friendly summary you can use:

Medicines (what they do + what to watch for)
Furosemide (Lasix) – “water pill”: helps kidneys remove extra salt and water, so swelling and breathlessness improve. Patients should expect periodic blood tests for electrolytes/kidney function [PocketDoktor]. (In UCG, furosemide is used for congestion; dosing is adjusted “according to response” at facility level) [UCG 4.1.3].
Enalapril (ACE inhibitor): opens blood vessels and reduces salt/water retention, making it easier for the heart to pump [PocketDoktor]. Watch for low BP symptoms (dizziness/lightheadedness); ACE inhibitors can cause a dry cough [PocketDoktor]. UCG: use with caution if SBP <90 and monitor renal function [UCG 4.1.3].
Carvedilol (beta blocker): blocks adrenaline so the heart doesn’t have to work as hard or beat as fast. Important: do not stop suddenly unless a doctor tells him to [PocketDoktor]. Watch for symptoms of low BP or low heart rate [PocketDoktor]. UCG notes beta blockers are added when patient is stable, with gradual increases [UCG 4.1.3].
Spironolactone: blocks stress hormones that make the heart stiff and scarred; patients should expect ongoing bloodwork for kidney function and potassium [PocketDoktor]. (UCG includes spironolactone as an additional medicine in chronic HF) [UCG 4.1.3].

Safety rule for today’s visit: if you are considering any dose change (increase/decrease/hold/switch, including “extra” diuretic), this change requires cardiologist confirmation before administration (or physician authorization if no cardiologist onsite). [HF Algorithm]

Lifestyle: salt, fluids, alcohol, activity
Salt: advise a low-salt diet; avoid packaged foods and avoid adding salt during cooking [PocketDoktor]. UCG also recommends reducing salt intake [UCG 4.1.3].
Fluids: advise him to discuss with the clinician how much to drink; in more severe HF he may be told to drink ~1–1.5 L/day [PocketDoktor; UCG 4.1.3].
Alcohol: avoid/stop alcohol—it can worsen heart failure [PocketDoktor; HF Algorithm].
Activity/exercise: encourage regular exercise within symptom limits; avoid overexertion and build gradually (and discuss before starting a new program) [PocketDoktor; UCG 4.1.3].
Home monitoring (simple “when to seek help”)
If he can, weigh in the morning; seek care if ≥1 kg overnight or 2–3 kg in a week, or if swelling/breathlessness/dizziness worsen [PocketDoktor].
Use the traffic-light concept: new/worsening breathlessness at rest, rapid weight gain, fainting, or worsening swelling = “Seek Help” [PocketDoktor].
