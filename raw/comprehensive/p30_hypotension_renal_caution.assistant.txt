A colleague suggests increasing carvedilol and enalapril because the patient has HFrEF. Is that safe with this BP and creatinine trend?

smart_toy

⚠️ URGENT: With SBP 82 mmHg + dizziness + cool peripheries and a rising creatinine, this is a red-flag low-perfusion state (possible cardiogenic shock vs hypovolaemia/other shock) and is not the moment to up‑titrate GDMT. The HF Algorithm flags cardiogenic shock (hypotension/poor perfusion) as requiring urgent attention [HF Algorithm], and UCG lists cold extremities + low BP as shock features [UCG 1.1.2].

Single next step: Escalate immediately for physician-led emergency management/transfer (call the receiving hospital/medical officer now and arrange urgent referral/ambulance) before any HF medication dose increases [HF Algorithm; UCG 1.1.2].
And to be explicit: any medication change (including increasing, holding, or stopping enalapril/carvedilol/spironolactone/furosemide) requires cardiologist confirmation before administration.

Medication safety issues to keep in mind before changing HF meds (from loaded guidance):

Do NOT increase enalapril or carvedilol when SBP <90 mmHg / symptomatic hypotension. UCG cautions ACE inhibitors and beta blockers when SBP <90 mmHg and says to monitor renal function [UCG 4.1.3]. GDMT tables require SBP >90 mmHg for enalapril titration and list symptomatic hypotension as a hold criterion [GDMT Tables]. Carvedilol titration also requires SBP >90 mmHg [GDMT Tables].
Renal deterioration: For enalapril, GDMT tables say hold if creatinine rises >30% from baseline and monitor Cr/K within 1–2 weeks after initiation/dose change [GDMT Tables]. (You’ve already noted creatinine rising—this increases risk with RAAS blockade.)
Spironolactone safety: requires eGFR >30 and K <5.0 to use safely, and hold if K ≥5.5, with close K/Cr monitoring [GDMT Tables]. With AKI/rising creatinine, hyperkalaemia risk increases (general clinical principle).
Beta blocker in decompensation/volume overload: GDMT tables caution do not uptitrate carvedilol if volume overloaded/decompensated [GDMT Tables].

Key clarification to guide the escalation/initial stabilization call:
What are her heart rate and rhythm (regular/irregular, ECG if available) and her current SpO₂/respiratory status right now? This helps distinguish shock type and urgency (e.g., ongoing pulmonary oedema/hypoxaemia vs primarily low-output).
