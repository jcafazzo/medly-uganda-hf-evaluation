Lifestyle priorities
Salt: “Salt makes your body hold onto water. Less salt helps reduce swelling and breathlessness.” UCG recommends reducing salt intake [UCG 4.1.3]; PocketDoktor advises a low-salt, low-fat diet and avoiding adding salt/packaged foods [PocketDoktor].
Fluids: “Too much fluid can worsen swelling and breathing.” UCG advises limit fluid to about 1–1.5 L/day in HF [UCG 4.1.3]. PocketDoktor notes some patients may be advised <1.5 L/day depending on severity [PocketDoktor].
Alcohol: “Alcohol can weaken the heart muscle and worsen heart failure.” UCG advises limit alcohol [UCG 4.1.3]; PocketDoktor advises avoid alcohol [PocketDoktor].
Activity: “Regular gentle exercise is good, but do not overexert—stop if symptoms worsen.” UCG recommends regular exercise within symptom limits [UCG 4.1.3]; PocketDoktor encourages exercise and discussing an exercise plan with the clinician [PocketDoktor].
Monitoring at home: UCG recommends periodic monitoring of weight, BP, heart rate, respiratory rate and oxygen saturation [UCG 4.1.3]. PocketDoktor teaches daily symptom/weight monitoring and seeking care for rapid weight gain (≥1 kg overnight or 2–3 kg/week) or worsening symptoms [PocketDoktor].
What each medicine is doing (and what to watch for)
Furosemide (Lasix) — “water pill”: “Helps your kidneys remove extra water and salt, reducing swollen legs and breathlessness.” Expect blood tests sometimes to monitor salts/kidney function [PocketDoktor].
Enalapril (ACE inhibitor): “Opens blood vessels and reduces salt and water retention, making it easier for your heart to pump.” Watch for low blood pressure symptoms; ACE‑I can cause a dry cough [PocketDoktor].
Carvedilol (beta blocker): “Blocks adrenaline so the heart doesn’t beat too fast or work too hard; helps the heart pump better over time.” Watch for low blood pressure or slow pulse symptoms and do not stop suddenly unless a doctor instructs [PocketDoktor].
Spironolactone: “Blocks stress hormones that cause scarring/stiffness in the heart.” Explain that he will need blood checks for kidney function and potassium [PocketDoktor]. (Clinically, this is especially important if kidney function worsens.)
