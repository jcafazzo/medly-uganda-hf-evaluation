face

A colleague suggests increasing carvedilol and enalapril because the patient has HFrEF. Is that safe with this BP and creatinine trend?

smart_toy

With SBP 82/48 and rising creatinine, it is not safe to up‑titrate enalapril or carvedilol right now.

Why (from the references you loaded):

UCG explicitly cautions: use ACE inhibitors and beta blockers with caution if SBP <90 mmHg and monitor renal function [UCG 4.1.3].
The GDMT Tables specify hemodynamic requirements for titration: enalapril requires SBP >90 mmHg and should be held if creatinine rises >30% from baseline; carvedilol requires SBP >90 mmHg and should not be uptitrated if decompensated/volume overloaded [GDMT Tables].
The HF Algorithm emphasizes beta blockers should be started/titrated when euvolemic (not acutely decompensated) [HF Algorithm].
Rising creatinine can reflect compromised renal perfusion from heart failure/shock, and UCG ARF management highlights referral/escalation for appropriate management when kidney function worsens [UCG 7.1.1].

Medication safety issues to flag before any changes

Up‑titration risk: Both enalapril and carvedilol titration is not appropriate when SBP is below 90 [UCG 4.1.3], [GDMT Tables].
Renal safety: With a creatinine rise, ACEi dose increase is unsafe; per GDMT Tables, consider hold criteria if Cr ↑ >30% and monitor K/Cr closely [GDMT Tables].
MRA (spironolactone) risk: needs K/Cr monitoring and has a hold threshold (K ≥5.5); also requires adequate kidney function (eGFR >30) [GDMT Tables].
Diuretic/volume status: if she is actually intravascularly depleted (over-diuresed) vs congested, further diuresis could worsen hypotension/AKI (general clinical principle). UCG ARF section notes oliguria may be treated with IV furosemide “according to response,” but this is in a referred/hospital context and depends on the underlying cause [UCG 7.1.1].

Escalation step to do before changing HF medicines Because this looks like shock/poor perfusion (cool peripheries + SBP 82/48 + AKI trend), the priority is urgent physician-led escalation/admission/transfer (RRH/ICU-capable if available) for stabilization and determination of whether this is cardiogenic shock vs hypovolaemia/other cause (general clinical principle). UCG ARF guidance supports referral when kidney function is worsening/no response to measures [UCG 7.1.1].

Important: Any medication change (increase, decrease, hold, or extra doses—including enalapril, carvedilol, spironolactone, or furosemide) requires cardiologist/physician confirmation before administration.

Focused question (needed to decide what to communicate urgently to the receiving team):
Do you have her current heart rate and SpO₂, and is she clinically congested right now (basal crepitations, raised JVP, pulmonary oedema) or more dry (no crackles, poor skin turgor, low JVP)?
