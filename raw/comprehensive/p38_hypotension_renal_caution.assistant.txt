A colleague suggests increasing carvedilol and enalapril because the patient has HFrEF. Is that safe with this BP and creatinine trend?

smart_toy

With SBP 84/50 + cool peripheries + rising creatinine, it is not safe to up‑titrate enalapril or carvedilol now—this is a red-flag low-perfusion state (possible cardiogenic shock or severe decompensation). The HF guidance specifically cautions that ACE inhibitors and beta blockers should be used with caution when SBP <90 mmHg and renal function monitored [UCG 4.1.3], and the GDMT tables require SBP >90 mmHg before ACE‑i up‑titration and also before carvedilol up‑titration (and advise not to uptitrate beta blockers if decompensated/volume overloaded) [GDMT Tables]. Her creatinine rise also triggers the GDMT “hold/avoid” criteria for ACE‑i if Cr increases >30% from baseline [GDMT Tables].

Single next step (before changing any HF medicines): escalate immediately for physician-led emergency management/transfer (treat as shock/poor perfusion) and do not make any HF med dose increases while you arrange this. Given the hemodynamics, any medication change (including holding or reducing enalapril/carvedilol/spironolactone or giving extra diuretic/vasodilator) requires cardiologist (or receiving physician) confirmation before administration.

To determine whether RAAS agents/MRA/diuretics should be held vs continued during stabilization, the most critical missing data is: What is her current heart rate/rhythm, SpO₂, and urine output (ml/hr if catheterized), and what is the % creatinine rise from baseline (baseline Cr → current Cr) and current potassium?
(These directly affect the GDMT hold criteria for ACE‑i/MRA and help distinguish hypovolaemia vs cardiogenic shock vs mixed states.) [GDMT Tables], [UCG 7.1.1]
