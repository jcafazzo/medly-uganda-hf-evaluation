For David, you can explain that heart failure means the heart is not pumping blood forward fast enough, so fluid can back up into the lungs (causing breathlessness) and/or into the legs and belly (causing swelling) [PocketDoktor]. The goals of treatment are to remove extra fluid, reduce the workload on the heart, and protect the heart from getting weaker over time [PocketDoktor; HF Algorithm].

One immediate safety check before counseling about “ongoing” medicines: can you confirm his current BP and symptoms today—specifically, is he dizzy now or are his hands/feet cool? If SBP <90 mmHg, UCG advises caution with ACE inhibitors and beta blockers and to monitor renal function, so this affects what you tell him about dose increases today [UCG 4.1.3]. And any medication change requires cardiologist confirmation before administration.

What to tell the patient about each medicine (plain language)

1) Furosemide (Lasix) — “water pill”

“This medicine helps your kidneys pass out extra salt and water, so swelling and breathlessness improve.” [PocketDoktor]
“Because it makes you pass urine, we often do blood tests to check salts and kidney function.” [PocketDoktor]
Clinically, the HF algorithm uses loop diuretics when the patient is hypervolemic (swollen legs, lung congestion) [HF Algorithm].

2) Enalapril (ACE inhibitor)

“This medicine opens blood vessels and helps reduce salt and water retention, so the heart pumps more easily.” [PocketDoktor]
“If you feel symptoms of low blood pressure (lightheadedness), tell us.” [PocketDoktor]
“A dry cough can happen with this type of medicine.” [PocketDoktor]

3) Carvedilol (beta blocker)

“This blocks adrenaline so the heart doesn’t have to work as hard or beat as fast.” [PocketDoktor]
“It can cause low pulse or low blood pressure symptoms.” [PocketDoktor]
“Do not stop it suddenly unless the doctor tells you—stopping suddenly can make the heart race.” [PocketDoktor]
Clinically, the HF algorithm emphasizes beta blockers are titrated toward a goal HR 60–80 bpm when stable [HF Algorithm].

4) Spironolactone (MRA)

“This blocks stress hormones that make the heart stiff and scarred.” [PocketDoktor]
“We will keep checking your kidney function and potassium with blood tests.” [PocketDoktor]
In the HF algorithm, spironolactone is part of the core regimen added after ACEi/ARB and beta blocker are established [HF Algorithm].
Lifestyle changes to emphasize (patient-friendly)

Salt

“Salt makes the body hold onto water—less salt means less swelling and less breathlessness.” [UCG 4.1.3; PocketDoktor]
Practical wording: “Avoid adding salt when cooking or at the table; avoid packaged foods.” [PocketDoktor]

Fluids

“Drinking too much can worsen fluid overload; your doctor may advise limiting fluids—often 1–1.5 L/day in heart failure.” [UCG 4.1.3; PocketDoktor]
Clarify: “This limit is individualized—follow the plan we write for you.” [PocketDoktor]

Alcohol

“Avoid alcohol—alcohol can worsen heart failure.” [PocketDoktor]

Activity

“Do regular activity/exercise within your symptom limits—avoid overexertion; we’ll guide you on a safe level.” [UCG 4.1.3; PocketDoktor]

Self-monitoring

Teach the key warning pattern: “If you gain 1 kg overnight or 2–3 kg in a week, or your breathing/swelling worsens, come back/seek help.” [PocketDoktor]
Use the traffic-light message: no worsening symptoms = doing well; new/worsening SOB, swelling, dizziness = pay attention; SOB at rest, fainting, rapid weight gain = seek help urgently [PocketDoktor].
