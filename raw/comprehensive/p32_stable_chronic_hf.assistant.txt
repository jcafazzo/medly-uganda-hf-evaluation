For John with chronic HFrEF (EF 35%), the key message is: heart failure is long-term, and control depends on (1) medicines, (2) diet/fluids, and (3) weight/symptom monitoring [PocketDoktor]. Before counseling, the single next step in clinic is to confirm he is stable today (no acute pulmonary oedema or shock) by checking BP, pulse/HR, SpO₂, and weight, and examining for lung crackles/JVP [HF Algorithm; UCG 4.1.3].

What are his current BP, HR, SpO₂, and weight change, and on exam does he have crackles or raised JVP today? (This determines whether we can counsel as “stable chronic HF” vs need acute management.) [HF Algorithm; UCG 4.1.3]

If he is stable, here is a patient-friendly summary you can give him:

Lifestyle that matters most
Salt: “Salt makes the body hold water, which worsens swelling and breathlessness.” Advise reduce salt (avoid adding salt; avoid packaged/fast foods) [UCG 4.1.3; PocketDoktor].
Fluids: In chronic HF, advise limit fluids to about 1–1.5 L/day if the clinician has advised restriction (especially if swelling/breathlessness worsen) [UCG 4.1.3; PocketDoktor].
Alcohol & smoking: Advise limit/avoid alcohol and stop smoking because both can worsen heart failure [UCG 4.1.3; PocketDoktor].
Activity/exercise: Encourage regular exercise within his symptom limits—stay active but avoid overexertion [UCG 4.1.3; PocketDoktor].
Daily monitoring: If he has a scale, encourage daily morning weight, and to seek care if weight rises ≥1 kg overnight or 2–3 kg in a week, or if breathing/swelling/dizziness worsens [PocketDoktor]. Also reinforce periodic clinic monitoring of weight, BP, HR, RR, SpO₂ [UCG 4.1.3].
What each medicine is for (simple explanations)
Furosemide (Lasix) – “water pill”: helps kidneys remove extra salt/water to reduce swelling and breathlessness; he should expect periodic blood tests for electrolytes/kidney function [PocketDoktor].
Enalapril (ACE inhibitor): “opens blood vessels and reduces salt/water retention, making it easier for the heart to pump” [PocketDoktor].
Carvedilol (beta blocker): “blocks adrenaline so the heart does not have to work as hard or beat as fast.” Important: do not stop suddenly unless told by a doctor [PocketDoktor].
Spironolactone (MRA): helps counter harmful stress hormones and reduces scarring/stiffness; he should expect ongoing bloodwork for kidney function and potassium [PocketDoktor].
Safety “warning signs” to teach him

Tell him to seek urgent care if he develops severe shortness of breath, severe dizziness/fainting, rapid weight gain, or worsening night-time breathing [PocketDoktor]. Also, in clinic we use caution with ACE inhibitors and beta blockers when systolic BP <90 mmHg, and we monitor kidney function [UCG 4.1.3].

Medication-change rule: if he asks “should I increase/stop any of these medicines?”, any starting/stopping/increasing/decreasing of HF medicines requires cardiologist confirmation before administration (or the responsible prescribing clinician).

You can find all these details in the Medications section of the Pocket Doktor.
