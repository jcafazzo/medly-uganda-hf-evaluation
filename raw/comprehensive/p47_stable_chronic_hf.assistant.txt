If he is stable, here is a patient-friendly explanation of the key medicines and lifestyle actions you can use in clinic:

1) Core medicines (what each one does + what to watch for)

Furosemide (Lasix) – “water tablet”: helps the kidneys remove extra salt and water, so swelling and breathlessness improve. Expect periodic blood tests for kidney function/electrolytes. [PocketDoktor]
Enalapril (ACE inhibitor) – “opens blood vessels and reduces fluid retention”: helps the heart pump more easily and reduces salt/water retention. Watch for low blood pressure symptoms (dizziness). A dry cough can happen with ACE inhibitors. [PocketDoktor]
Carvedilol (beta blocker) – “slows the heart and reduces strain”: blocks adrenaline so the heart doesn’t have to work as hard or beat as fast. Warn him: do not stop it suddenly unless a doctor tells him—his heart may race. Watch for dizziness or very slow pulse. [PocketDoktor]
Spironolactone (Aldactone) – “blocks harmful stress hormones”: helps prevent the heart becoming stiff and scarred; requires ongoing bloodwork for kidney function and potassium. [PocketDoktor]

Clinical link to Uganda guidance: for chronic HF, UCG emphasizes continued treatment with these medicines, with doses progressively increased to achieve control, plus salt/fluid restriction and regular follow-up monitoring. [UCG 4.1.3] The HF Algorithm similarly prioritizes (for HFrEF EF<40%) loop diuretic if congested, ACEi/ARB/ARNi, beta blocker, spironolactone, and recommends adding an SGLT2 inhibitor in most HF patients. [HF Algorithm]

Important safety message for this patient: because he previously had very low BP and rising creatinine, any dose changes (increase/decrease/holding) to enalapril, carvedilol, spironolactone, or furosemide require cardiologist/physician confirmation before administration. [UCG 4.1.3]

2) Lifestyle actions to reduce fluid and prevent worsening

Salt: advise low-salt diet—avoid adding salt when cooking and avoid packaged foods. [PocketDoktor; UCG 4.1.3]
Fluids: many HF patients are advised to limit fluids, commonly ~1–1.5 L/day if told by the clinician. [UCG 4.1.3; PocketDoktor]
Alcohol and smoking: advise minimizing/avoiding alcohol and stopping tobacco—both can worsen HF. [PocketDoktor]
Activity: encourage regular exercise within symptom limits; avoid overexertion. [UCG 4.1.3; PocketDoktor]
