Focus the counseling on (1) why each medicine matters, (2) what symptoms mean “seek help now”, and (3) the daily habits that prevent fluid build‑up [HF Algorithm; UCG 4.1.3; PocketDoktor].

How to explain his key medicines (patient-friendly)

Furosemide (Lasix): a “water pill” that helps the kidneys remove extra salt and water to reduce swelling and breathlessness; he should expect periodic blood tests to check salts and kidney function [PocketDoktor]. In HF, loop diuretics are used when there are signs of excess fluid such as ankle swelling or lung congestion [HF Algorithm].
ACE inhibitor (Enalapril): opens blood vessels and reduces salt/water retention so the heart pumps more easily; watch for low blood pressure symptoms (dizziness) and some people develop a dry cough [PocketDoktor]. UCG advises starting low and increasing gradually only if tolerated, and to be cautious if SBP is low [UCG 4.1.3].
Beta blocker (Carvedilol): blocks adrenaline so the heart doesn’t work as hard or beat as fast; warn him not to stop suddenly unless a doctor tells him (the heart can race if stopped abruptly) and to watch for low BP/slow pulse symptoms [PocketDoktor]. Beta blockers are part of HFrEF/HFmrEF treatment and are titrated toward a goal heart rate 60–80 bpm when stable [HF Algorithm].
Spironolactone: blocks stress hormones that cause heart scarring/stiffness; explain that he will need ongoing blood tests for kidney function and potassium [PocketDoktor]. It is added as part of guideline therapy after ACEi/ARB and beta blocker are established [HF Algorithm].

Lifestyle priorities to reinforce

Salt & fluids: reduce salt and limit fluids to ~1–1.5 L/day if advised, because too much fluid can worsen heart failure [UCG 4.1.3; PocketDoktor].
Alcohol: minimize or avoid alcohol because it can worsen heart failure [PocketDoktor; HF Algorithm].
Activity: regular exercise within symptom limits (avoid overexertion) [UCG 4.1.3; PocketDoktor].
Daily monitoring: track weight/BP when possible; he should seek care if he gains ≥1 kg overnight or 2–3 kg in a week, or if breathing/swelling worsens [PocketDoktor]. (This also matches HF triggers like dietary indiscretion or missed meds) [HF Algorithm].
Medication adherence & supply: take medicines consistently and avoid running out—missed doses are a common trigger for worsening HF [HF Algorithm].

Safety “when to seek help urgently” (simple framing) Tell him to seek urgent care for severe shortness of breath (especially at rest), severe dizziness/fainting, or severe chest pain/pressure [PocketDoktor].
