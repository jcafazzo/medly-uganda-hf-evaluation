face

A colleague suggests increasing carvedilol and enalapril because the patient has HFrEF. Is that safe with this BP and creatinine trend?

smart_toy

With SBP 82/48 plus cool peripheries and rising creatinine, it is not safe to up‑titrate carvedilol or enalapril right now. Both UCG and the GDMT tables indicate these drugs should be used with caution/held from titration in hypotension and worsening renal function: UCG cautions ACE inhibitors and beta blockers when SBP <90 mmHg and to monitor renal function [UCG 4.1.3], and the GDMT tables specify SBP >90 mmHg for enalapril initiation/titration and carvedilol hemodynamic requirements SBP >90 mmHg (and HR >55), plus ACEi hold consideration if Cr rises >30% [GDMT Tables]. Also, beta blockers should not be up‑titrated if decompensated/volume overloaded [GDMT Tables].

Medication safety issues to flag before any HF medicine change

Proposed up‑titration in shock/hypoperfusion is high risk (may worsen hypotension/perfusion) [UCG 4.1.3], [GDMT Tables].
Worsening renal function: ACEi/ARB/ARNI require Cr/K monitoring and GDMT tables advise holding if Cr ↑ >30%; spironolactone requires close K/Cr monitoring and is held if K ≥5.5 [GDMT Tables]. (Hyperkalaemia risk is a general clinical principle.)
Any dose increase/decrease/holding is a medication change and requires cardiologist confirmation before administration. Even if you think the right move is to hold or reduce, discuss with the cardiologist/senior physician first.

Single next step (most urgent): treat this as possible cardiogenic shock/AKI and escalate immediately for urgent physician-led review and referral/transfer, while you obtain a quick perfusion profile to guide that escalation (mental status, HR/rhythm, capillary refill, urine output) [HF Algorithm] (red flags: cardiogenic shock), and ensure renal function/electrolytes are checked [HF Algorithm].

To decide whether he is primarily “cold and wet” (congested + hypoperfused) vs another shock cause, and to frame the call to the receiving unit: what is his heart rate/rhythm (ECG if available) and what is his urine output in the last 1–2 hours (or is he anuric)?
