Single next step (do now): Arrange immediate transfer to the nearest facility/ED (HC4 or Hospital) and alert the receiving clinician for suspected acute pulmonary oedema [UCG 4.1.4]. While she is waiting for transport, advise her to sit upright/propped up (not lying flat) [UCG 4.1.4].

Escalation threshold (exact, from UCG hypoxaemia guidance):

If she has a pulse oximeter and SpO₂ < 90%, that is hypoxaemia and a life‑threatening emergency requiring urgent oxygen and higher-level care [UCG 1.4].
Even without an SpO₂ reading, danger signs like inability to talk in full sentences, marked difficulty breathing, or confusion indicate emergency oxygen/urgent care irrespective of SpO₂ [UCG 1.4].
The UCG pulmonary oedema section flags severe dyspnoea/rapid breathing and cough with frothy blood‑stained sputum as key clinical features—presence of these warrants urgent acute management at facility level [UCG 4.1.4].

Medication changes: In pulmonary oedema, acute treatments may include oxygen and IV/IM diuretics (e.g., furosemide) per UCG, but any medication change (including an extra dose at home or changing her furosemide) requires cardiologist confirmation before administration [UCG 4.1.4].

One key piece of information to decide how urgent the transport needs to be (ambulance vs immediate self/escort): Right now, can she speak in full sentences at rest, and does she have a current SpO₂ (or at least a home BP if available)? [UCG 1.4]
