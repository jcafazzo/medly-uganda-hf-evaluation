smart_toy

With SBP 86 mmHg plus dizziness and cool peripheries, it is not safe to up‑titrate enalapril or carvedilol right now. Both UCG and the titration tables emphasize caution/holding rather than increasing when BP is <90 mmHg and when renal function is worsening.

Single next step (before any HF medicine changes):
Urgently escalate to a senior clinician/receiving hospital physician now (phone) for shock management and to confirm any medication holds/adjustments. This is a red-flag scenario (possible cardiogenic shock/poor perfusion + AKI), and medication changes require cardiologist confirmation before administration. UCG flags hypotension <90 mmHg as a situation to use ACE inhibitors and beta blockers with caution and to monitor renal function [UCG 4.1.3], and the GDMT tables specify hemodynamic requirements (generally SBP >90) and hold criteria with creatinine rise for enalapril [GDMT Tables].

Key safety issues to raise on escalation (so the physician can decide what to hold/change):

Enalapril (ACEi): GDMT tables—hold if creatinine rises >30% or K ≥5.5, and requires SBP >90 mmHg for titration; UCG cautions ACEi use when SBP <90 and to monitor renal function [GDMT Tables; UCG 4.1.3].
Carvedilol (beta blocker): initiation/titration requires SBP >90 (and HR >55) and avoid uptitration if decompensated/volume overloaded [GDMT Tables]. UCG also cautions beta blockers when SBP <90 [UCG 4.1.3].
Spironolactone (MRA): needs close K/Cr monitoring; hold if K ≥5.5 [GDMT Tables].
Furosemide: if she’s hypotensive with rising creatinine, she could be over-diuresed/hypovolaemic or in low-output HF; direction of change depends on volume status and must be physician-led. (Any change requires cardiologist confirmation.)

To help determine whether this is more likely cardiogenic shock vs hypovolaemia/over-diuresis, what is her heart rate and urine output (or when last passed urine), and on exam is she congested (raised JVP/basal creps/oedema) or “dry” (flat JVP, no creps, poor skin turgor)?
