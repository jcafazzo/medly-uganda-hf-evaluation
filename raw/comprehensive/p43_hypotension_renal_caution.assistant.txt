face

A colleague suggests increasing carvedilol and enalapril because the patient has HFrEF. Is that safe with this BP and creatinine trend?

smart_toy

With SBP 84/50 + dizziness + cool peripheries + rising creatinine, it is not safe to up‑titrate enalapril or carvedilol now. Both UCG and the GDMT tables indicate these agents should be used with caution/held from uptitration when SBP <90 mmHg, and ACEi titration in particular requires close renal monitoring and should be held with a significant creatinine rise [UCG 4.1.3; GDMT Tables]. Also, the HF Algorithm notes beta blockers should be started/titrated when euvolemic (not acutely decompensated) [HF Algorithm].

Medication safety issues to flag before any HF medicine change (and to communicate in referral/consult):

ACE inhibitor (enalapril): hypotension + rising creatinine → risk of worsening renal function; GDMT tables: hold/avoid if symptomatic hypotension and hold if creatinine rises >30% or K ≥5.5 [GDMT Tables]. UCG: use ACEi with caution if SBP <90 and monitor renal function [UCG 4.1.3].
Beta blocker (carvedilol): GDMT tables require SBP >90 mmHg and avoid uptitration if decompensated/volume overloaded [GDMT Tables]; HF Algorithm: titrate when euvolemic [HF Algorithm].
Spironolactone: requires kidney function and potassium safety thresholds; GDMT tables: use when eGFR >30 and K <5.0, and hold if K ≥5.5 with close K/Cr monitoring [GDMT Tables].
Furosemide: in the setting of hypotension/AKI, over-diuresis can worsen perfusion; conversely, congestion may require diuresis—this needs clinician review with vitals, volume status, and labs. Any dose change is still a medication change.

Escalation/level of care: This presentation suggests shock/poor perfusion (cold extremities, low BP) which the UCG treats as an emergency requiring urgent senior/physician management and monitoring (BP, HR, RR, capillary refill, mental state, urine output) and urgent referral if beyond facility capacity [UCG 1.1.2].

Critical rule for you as the nurse: any decision to increase, decrease, or hold enalapril/carvedilol/spironolactone/furosemide is a medication change and requires cardiologist confirmation before administration (or the receiving hospital physician if cardiologist is not available on site).

Single next step: treat this as possible shock/AKI and escalate immediately to the most senior clinician available and initiate urgent referral/transfer, while documenting perfusion markers per UCG shock assessment [UCG 1.1.2].

To guide the safest immediate stabilization and what to tell the receiving team: what is her current heart rate and oxygen saturation, and do you have her potassium value and the exact creatinine numbers (baseline and current) to judge whether the rise is >30%? [GDMT Tables]
