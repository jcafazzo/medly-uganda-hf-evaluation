A colleague suggests increasing carvedilol and enalapril because the patient has HFrEF. Is that safe with this BP and creatinine trend?

smart_toy

No—with SBP 84/50 and rising creatinine, up‑titrating carvedilol or enalapril is not safe right now. Both UCG and the GDMT titration tables require SBP >90 mmHg before ACE‑i up‑titration, and carvedilol up‑titration also requires SBP >90 (and HR >55); UCG additionally cautions ACE‑i and beta blockers when SBP <90 and to monitor renal function [UCG 4.1.3; GDMT Tables]. This looks like a red-flag decompensation/poor perfusion state (cool peripheries, hypotension, AKI), so escalation is the priority [HF Algorithm].

Single next step (do now): call for urgent senior review/transfer as shock is possible. Activate your facility emergency pathway and arrange immediate physician/medical officer review and prepare urgent referral/ambulance if you can’t stabilize on-site—this is a “red flag requiring urgent attention” in the HF algorithm [HF Algorithm].

Important medication safety issues to keep in mind before any HF medicine changes:

Do not up‑titrate:
Enalapril when SBP ≤90 or if creatinine has risen significantly (tables: hold if Cr ↑ >30%) [GDMT Tables].
Carvedilol when SBP ≤90 and do not up‑titrate if decompensated/volume overloaded [GDMT Tables].
Spironolactone: requires close K/Cr monitoring and should be held if K ≥5.5 (and generally requires adequate renal function) [GDMT Tables].
Any dose change/holding/starting of enalapril, carvedilol, spironolactone, or furosemide requires cardiologist/physician confirmation before administration. (Even if the change is to “hold”.)
