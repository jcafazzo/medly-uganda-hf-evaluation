Both UCG and the GDMT titration tables specify SBP >90 mmHg as a requirement for ACE inhibitor and carvedilol titration, and UCG explicitly cautions ACE inhibitors and beta blockers when SBP <90 mmHg, with renal-function monitoring [UCG 4.1.3], [GDMT Tables]. In addition, the GDMT tables advise holding ACEi if creatinine rises >30% from baseline [GDMT Tables].

Single next step (do now, before any HF medicine changes):
Treat this as possible cardiogenic shock/poor perfusion and escalate immediately to a physician/medical officer for urgent review and referral/transfer planning (hospital level). This is urgent given hypotension + cool peripheries + worsening renal function [HF Algorithm] (red flag: cardiogenic shock), [UCG 7.1.1] (shock/heart failure can cause acute renal failure and needs higher-level management).

Medication safety issues to explicitly flag to the prescriber (before changing anything):

Do not uptitrate carvedilol when hypotensive; carvedilol titration requires SBP >90 and HR >55, and avoid uptitration if decompensated [GDMT Tables].
Do not uptitrate enalapril when SBP <90; enalapril titration requires SBP >90, and ACEi should be used with caution in low BP with renal monitoring [UCG 4.1.3], [GDMT Tables].
Creatinine rise: consider that ACEi may need to be held if creatinine has increased >30% from baseline [GDMT Tables]. (Any holding is a medication change and needs confirmation—see below.)
Spironolactone safety: MRAs require close K/Cr monitoring and are held if K ≥5.5 [GDMT Tables]. (You haven’t given potassium yet.)

Critical rule for you as nurse: any change—holding, reducing, or increasing enalapril/carvedilol/spironolactone/furosemide—requires physician/cardiologist confirmation before administration.
