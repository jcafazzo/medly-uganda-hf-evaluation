What matters most day-to-day Your heart is not pumping blood forward fast enough, so fluid can build up in the lungs (causing breathlessness) and in the legs/ankles (causing swelling) [PocketDoktor]. Lifestyle: salt, fluids, alcohol, activity

Salt: “Salt makes the body hold onto water.” Advise reduce salt and avoid adding extra salt and salty/packaged foods [UCG 4.1.3], [PocketDoktor].
Fluids: “Too much fluid can worsen swelling/breathlessness.” If the clinician has advised it, keep fluids around 1–1.5 L/day [UCG 4.1.3], and confirm the exact goal with the doctor based on his current status [PocketDoktor].
Alcohol & smoking: Encourage avoid/limit alcohol and stop smoking (both worsen heart failure) [PocketDoktor]; UCG also advises limit alcohol [UCG 4.1.3].
Activity: “Do regular gentle exercise within your limits—don’t overexert.” Agree on a plan with the clinician, especially if symptoms increase with exertion [UCG 4.1.3], [PocketDoktor].

How to describe each medicine (and what he should watch for)
(Use the patient’s actual pill names.)

Furosemide (Lasix) — ‘water pill’: Helps kidneys remove extra salt and water, so swelling and breathlessness improve [PocketDoktor]. UCG uses furosemide for HF symptom control and adjusts dose to response [UCG 4.1.3].
What to watch: more urination is expected; he may need periodic blood tests for salts/kidney function [PocketDoktor].

Enalapril — ACE inhibitor: “Opens blood vessels and reduces salt/water retention so the heart pumps more easily” [PocketDoktor]. HF algorithm lists ACEi as core therapy for HFrEF/HFmrEF with titration toward goal dose [HF Algorithm].
What to watch: symptoms of low blood pressure (dizziness, near-fainting); ACEi can cause a dry cough [PocketDoktor].

Carvedilol — beta blocker: “Blocks adrenaline so the heart doesn’t have to work as hard or beat as fast” [PocketDoktor]. It’s part of foundational therapy in HFrEF/HFmrEF when stable and titrated to a goal heart-rate range [HF Algorithm].
What to watch: dizziness or very slow pulse; do not stop suddenly unless a doctor tells him to [PocketDoktor].

Spironolactone — ‘aldosterone blocker’: Helps block stress hormones that stiffen the heart and cause scarring [PocketDoktor]. It is included as add-on therapy in the HF algorithm plan after ACEi/ARB and beta blocker [HF Algorithm].
What to watch: he will need ongoing kidney function and potassium checks [PocketDoktor].

Monitoring at home (what you want him to do)

Encourage daily weight if he has a scale; he should seek care if he gains ≥1 kg overnight or 2–3 kg in a week, or if breathlessness/swelling/dizziness worsens [PocketDoktor].
At clinic, UCG recommends periodic monitoring of weight, BP, heart rate, respiratory rate, and SpO₂ [UCG 4.1.3].

Safety note (important): if he reports severe shortness of breath, severe chest pain/pressure, fainting, or severe dizziness, he should return to hospital immediately [PocketDoktor].

Because any adjustment of doses (including “extra Lasix,” reducing, holding, or increasing any HF medicine) is a medication change, this change requires cardiologist/doctor confirmation before administration.

One question to tailor the salt/fluid and symptom plan: does David currently have any “seek help” symptoms today—shortness of breath at rest/while seated, fainting, or rapid weight gain—or is it mainly mild ankle swelling only? [PocketDoktor]
