A colleague suggests increasing carvedilol and enalapril because the patient has HFrEF. Is that safe with this BP and creatinine trend?

smart_toy

With SBP 88 mmHg + dizziness + cool peripheries and rising creatinine, it is not safe to up‑titrate enalapril or carvedilol now. Both UCG and the GDMT titration tables require SBP >90 mmHg for ACE‑inhibitor/beta‑blocker titration, and UCG explicitly cautions to use ACE inhibitors and beta blockers with caution when SBP <90 and to monitor renal function [UCG 4.1.3; GDMT Tables]. For enalapril specifically, the GDMT tables also advise holding if creatinine rises >30% from baseline and monitoring Cr/K within 1–2 weeks after changes [GDMT Tables].

Medication safety issues to flag before any HF med changes

Hypotension/hypoperfusion: uptitrating ACEi or carvedilol in symptomatic hypotension can worsen shock physiology; carvedilol titration requires SBP >90 and HR >55 [GDMT Tables].
Worsening renal function: ACEi/ARB/ARNI require close renal/K monitoring; enalapril: hold if Cr ↑ >30% [GDMT Tables].
MRA (spironolactone) renal/K risk: spironolactone requires eGFR >30 and K <5.0; hold if K ≥5.5, with early repeat K/Cr monitoring after initiation or changes [GDMT Tables].
Possible AKI in HF/shock: AKI can be due to compromised renal perfusion from HF/shock and needs urgent higher-level management; UCG recommends starting hospital management and referring to higher level for more appropriate management, especially if worsening function/anuria [UCG 7.1.1].
Avoid nephrotoxins: in renal impairment, avoid/stop nephrotoxic drugs like NSAIDs; UCG emphasizes avoiding drugs that worsen kidney damage [UCG 7.1.2; UCG 7.1.1].

Escalation step to take before changing any HF medicines (now): treat this as possible cardiogenic shock/AKI and escalate immediately to senior clinician/receiving hospital team for urgent transfer, while monitoring urine output and vitals per AKI guidance [UCG 7.1.1].

Any action to hold, reduce, or increase enalapril/carvedilol/spironolactone/furosemide (or to give IV diuretics/inotropes) is a medication change—this requires cardiologist/physician confirmation before administration.

To guide the next immediate decision (shock vs congestion predominance): what is his current SpO₂ and respiratory status (crackles/frothy sputum), heart rate, and urine output over the last 6–12 hours? [UCG 7.1.1; HF Algorithm]
