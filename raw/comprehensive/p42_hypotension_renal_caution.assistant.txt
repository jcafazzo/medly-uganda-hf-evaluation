face

A colleague suggests increasing carvedilol and enalapril because the patient has HFrEF. Is that safe with this BP and creatinine trend?

smart_toy

With SBP 88 mmHg, dizziness, cool peripheries, and a rising creatinine, this looks like poor perfusion/cardiogenic shock rather than a stable outpatient titration scenario. In that context, increasing carvedilol or enalapril is not safe to do empirically.

Single next step: do not up‑titrate GDMT now; urgently escalate to a senior clinician/receiving hospital for immediate review and transfer. Cardiogenic shock is a red flag requiring urgent attention [HF Algorithm]. Also, UCG cautions that ACE inhibitors and beta blockers should be used with caution when SBP <90 mmHg and renal function must be monitored [UCG 4.1.3]. Any dose change/holding/extra dosing requires cardiologist (or physician) confirmation before administration.

Key medication safety issues to highlight to the physician you escalate to (before any change):

Carvedilol (beta blocker): beta blockers should be started when the patient is euvolemic (not acutely decompensated) and titrated cautiously; GDMT tables require SBP >90 mmHg for titration and advise avoid uptitration if decompensated/volume overloaded [HF Algorithm; GDMT Tables]. With SBP 88 and shock features, do not increase without physician authorization.
Enalapril (ACE inhibitor): GDMT tables specify hemodynamic requirement SBP >90 mmHg and to hold if creatinine rises >30% (and monitor Cr/K within 1–2 weeks after changes) [GDMT Tables]. UCG also cautions ACEi use when SBP <90 and to monitor renal function [UCG 4.1.3]. So do not increase now without physician review.
Spironolactone (MRA): requires renal/K monitoring; GDMT tables: use only if eGFR >30 and K <5.0 and hold if K ≥5.5, with close K/Cr monitoring [GDMT Tables]. With rising creatinine and unknown K today, this is a safety concern to flag before any adjustments.
Furosemide: in pulmonary oedema, UCG uses IM/slow IV furosemide with repeat dosing according to response [UCG 4.1.4], but in hypotension/poor perfusion with renal dysfunction, diuretic decisions must be individualized by the receiving physician. Any extra dose/route change requires cardiologist/physician confirmation before administration.

To triage shock severity and inform the escalation call: what are his heart rate, SpO₂, mental status, and urine output right now?
